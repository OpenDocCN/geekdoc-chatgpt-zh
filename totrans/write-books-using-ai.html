Understanding Basic Commands and Prompts for AI

To communicate effectively with an AI writing tool, you will need to understand how to craft commands and prompts. These are the bread and butter of interacting with AI, dictating the quality and relevance of the content it generates.


Start Simple: Begin with basic commands to get a feel for how the AI responds. For instance, try 'Summarize the plot of Romeo and Juliet,' to assess the AI's summarization and storytelling skills and to see how the AI constructs sentences and paragraphs.


Be Clear and Concise: Ambiguity can lead to unexpected results. If you want a summary of a historical event, for example, specify "Summarize the key points of the Battle of Waterloo in three sentences."


Provide Context: When you need content that fits within a larger piece, give the AI background information. For instance, "Write a concluding paragraph for an article about renewable energy that emphasizes future technological advancements."


Iterate and Refine: Use the AI's responses to refine your prompts. If the content isn't quite right, adjust your command and try again. This could mean adding more detail or shifting the focus of the prompt.


The Anatomy of an AI Prompt

A prompt is your opening gambit in the chess game of content creation, a succinct yet potent phrase that sets the AI on a quest for literary gold.

Picture yourself as a cartographer sketching a map for your AI crew; a clear X marks the spot. "Write a story about a pirate," is your starting point, but the magic unfolds with a flourish of detail: "Write a thrilling pirate adventure story set in the Caribbean during the 18th century, featuring a cunning captain and a mysterious treasure map."


Command Structure: The Blueprint of Clarity

Your command is a blueprint, a structured guide for the AI to follow with architectural precision. It's a three-part harmony: the action, the subject, and the embellishments. "Describe a bustling medieval marketplace at dawn, focusing on the sights, sounds, and smells," is a command that paints a vivid scene for the AI to elaborate upon.


Keywords and Parameters: The Compass and Sextant

Keywords are your compass, guiding the AI through the thematic seas, while parameters set the boundaries of this exploratory voyage. A command transforms from "Write a poem" to "Write a sonnet about unrequited love using iambic pentameter," and suddenly, the AI is charting a course through the waters of poetic form and emotion.


Saving and Organizing: The Cartographer's Archive

Once your AI has unearthed treasures, you must stow them away like precious maps. Save and organize your AI-generated content with meticulous care, labeling each piece with a name that whispers of its contents, such as "PirateStory_18thCenturyCaribbean_20240106."


Iteration and Refinement: The Sculptor's Chisel

Your initial prompt is raw marble; each iteration is a stroke of the chisel, refining the form. If your pirate tale lacks suspense, a new command — "Intensify the conflict in the pirate story by introducing a rival treasure hunter" — sharpens the edges and adds depth.


Prompt Variations: The Playwright's Pen

The prompts you craft are the pen strokes of a playwright, each one designed to evoke a different scene on the stage of AI creativity. Aiming for a touch of humor? Cast your characters in a comedic light with a prompt like, "Write a humorous dialogue between a pirate and a parrot about sharing the ship's cabin."

Or, if the rich tapestry of your narrative requires more texture, you might nudge the AI with, "Describe the intricate details of the pirate ship's design, emphasizing the craftsmanship of the woodwork and the colors of the sails."


The Importance of Feedback Loops

In the dance of creation with AI, feedback loops are your tango. It's a partnership where each step, each twirl of feedback — be it an upvote, a downvote, or a comment — teaches the AI the rhythm of your preferences. Engage in this dance, guide your partner with cues like, "This is good, but amplify the emotional turmoil of the characters," and watch as the AI learns to match your tempo.


Command Syntax for Specific Genres

Each literary genre has its own secret handshake, a code of keywords and stylistic flourishes that you can share with the AI. For a sci-fi saga, sprinkle in terms like "interstellar travel," "quantum AI," or "dystopian society." A romance, on the other hand, may bloom under "forbidden love," "sweeping emotions," or "picturesque settings."


Combining Commands for Complexity

As you grow more confident, your commands can become more complex, like a chef's recipe gaining ingredients. "Write a dialogue between two star-crossed lovers from rival intergalactic factions, blending tender moments with the tension of their situation," is a prompt that weaves together character, conflict, and setting into a rich narrative fabric.


Experiment with Different Prompt Styles:

Directives: Tell the AI exactly what to do, such as "List the benefits of meditation."

Questions: Frame your prompt as a question to encourage a response, like "What are the main health benefits of a plant-based diet?"

Creative Challenges: Push the AI's capabilities with prompts that require imagination, "Write a short story about a robot discovering a hidden garden."


Leverage Keywords and Parameters:

Keywords: Include relevant keywords to guide the AI, such as "Write a blog introduction about sustainable fashion trends."

Parameters: Set parameters for length, tone, or style, like "Craft a witty post about coffee that's under 280 characters."



The Best Prompts for Writing a Book

You might think that writing a book using AI is easy.  It is - but perhaps not as easy as you think. 

Sure, you can get plenty of words onto the screen quickly using AI, but just because you have a ton of words doesn’t mean that you have a book.  I hope I’m not bursting your bubble here, but the truth is that you must still craft your book every step of the way, just as authors have been doing for thousands of years.

When using AI to help you write a book, this means that you must have suitable prompts to help you write engaging content in an organized fashion. In my experience, this means prompting AI section by section following a carefully crafted outline.

When I first began to use AI, I thought I could write a prompt that would yield a perfectly written chapter.  You can get a chapter, but the likelihood is that it won’t be very good.  I’ve since discovered that prompts need to be focused - section by section, chunk by chunk.


The best prompts are clear, specific, and structured with your requests.


Here are some tips to effectively use AI prompts in your writing process:

Define Your Goals: Before you start, know what you want to achieve with each prompt. Are you generating ideas, seeking help with an outline, looking for character development, or crafting dialogue?


Be Specific: The more detailed your prompt, the more tailored the AI's response will be. Instead of "Write a book," try "Write a synopsis for a science fiction novel about a time-traveling historian."


Break It Down: Just like the saying about eating an elephant one bite at a time, approach your book in sections. Work on prompts for chapters, scenes, character arcs, or plot points individually.


Iterate: Use the AI's responses as a starting point and refine with additional prompts. For example, if you're not satisfied with a character description, you can prompt the AI to expand on certain traits or backstories.


Combine Human and AI Strengths: Use AI for generating ideas, overcoming writer's block, or providing suggestions on structure. Then apply your creativity to infuse personality, depth, and coherence to the story.


Edit and Revise: AI-generated text often requires editing to match your voice and style. Use the AI's output as a draft and revise to ensure consistency and flow.


Use Multiple Prompts for Complex Tasks: If you're tackling a large aspect of your book, such as world-building, consider using a series of prompts to address different elements like geography, social structure, and history.


Keep It Conversational: Frame your prompts as if you're having a conversation with the AI. This can make the process feel more natural and may yield better results.


Monitor Consistency: AI can sometimes produce inconsistent content. Regularly check for continuity in your story and characters to maintain a cohesive narrative.

10 Tips to Using Prompts Effectively


1. Be Precise, Not Presumptuous: AI is not a mind reader; it thrives on explicit instructions. A vague command is the enemy of good results. For instance, "Write a story" is an open-ended request that can lead to a multitude of directions, which may not align with your expectations.

Instead, by asking the AI to "Craft a 500-word tale of a reluctant hero's journey set in a dystopian future," you provide a clear framework — genre, word count, character archetype, and setting — which guides the AI to generate content that is more likely to meet your specific needs.


2. Embrace the Details: Specificity is the soul of narrative. In the same way that rich, nuanced characters make for a compelling read, detailed prompts produce more tailored results.

If you're looking to generate a character, for example, the additional information about their fears, interests, and background gives the AI a much clearer picture to work with, resulting in a more fleshed-out and interesting creation.


3. Direct with Purpose: Your prompts should be imbued with intention, much like a strong, active voice in writing. Passive language can lead to passive results. Instructing the AI with a prompt like "Analyze the latest sales trends and project next quarter's growth," directs the AI to not only present data but also interpret it and predict future outcomes, providing a more comprehensive and actionable analysis.


4. Converse with Clarity: Think of your interaction with the AI as a conversation where clarity is key. Overly broad or ambiguous requests can lead to generic responses.

By asking for "three strategies to improve my focus while working from home," and acknowledging potential distractions, you're initiating a more focused and relevant dialogue that can yield practical and personalized advice.


5. Inject Personality - Sparingly: A little personality can go a long way in making the interaction with AI more enjoyable. However, it's important to strike a balance.

Humor and personality should not come at the expense of the clarity of the request. Use wit to enhance, not obscure, the intention of your prompt.


6. Command with Structure: Just as a novel is composed of individual sections that come together to form a cohesive whole, your prompts to the AI should be crafted for each specific part of your book. For example, when developing a chapter for a novel, create separate, detailed prompts for each key element.

Start with a prompt for the setting, such as "Describe a bustling medieval market with vivid sensory details to immerse the reader." Follow with a character prompt, like "Develop a dialogue that reveals the protagonist's cleverness and wit in negotiating."

Then, a conflict prompt could be, "Introduce an unexpected betrayal that disrupts the protagonist's plan, heightening tension." Lastly, a prompt for the chapter's conclusion might read, "Craft a cliffhanger that hints at a mysterious figure observing the events, enticing the reader to continue."

By breaking down the chapter into structured prompts for each section, you guide the AI to concentrate on each critical piece, ensuring depth and precision that seamlessly weave into your narrative tapestry.


7. Repeat for Emphasis: Repetition is a powerful rhetorical device that can reinforce key concepts and make your instructions stick. When you want to emphasize an idea in the content the AI generates, make it clear in your prompt.

By asking for a poem that echoes the theme of loss in every stanza, you're not just requesting a poem; you're sculpting its thematic core. The repetition guides the AI to focus on the motif, making the final piece resonate with the intended emotional depth.


8. Contradict to Deepen: Introducing a contradiction or a paradox in your prompt can lead to more nuanced and thought-provoking content.

For example, asking the AI to "Explain success through the lens of famous failures" invites it to explore the complex relationship between failure and success, potentially uncovering insights that a more straightforward prompt might not yield.

This approach can stimulate the AI to think in more abstract terms and deliver content that offers a fresh perspective.


9. End with Impact: The way you conclude your prompt should signal to the AI the desired outcome with precision and force. "Provide three actionable steps to elevate my writing, with examples for clarity," is a prompt that demands a conclusion with substance. It's not just asking for feedback; it's specifying the format and the need for practical application.

The AI is directed to wrap up its response in a way that leaves you with clear, implementable advice, much like a strong ending leaves a lasting impression in a reader's mind.


10. Economize Your Words: Brevity is the soul of wit, and in the case of AI prompts, it's also the key to efficiency. The goal is to convey your request without unnecessary verbiage that could confuse the AI or dilute the potency of your command. Each word should earn its place in your prompt, contributing to the clarity and precision of your request.

This doesn't mean that prompts must be dry or robotic; they can and should be engaging, but always with an eye toward conciseness.

Developing a Strong Outline

Creating a strong outline is a pivotal step in the book-writing process. It serves as a roadmap, guiding you from the beginning to the end of your writing journey.

Here's why a well-crafted outline is crucial and how it can enhance your book:

Provides Structure: A good outline gives your book a clear structure. It helps you organize your thoughts and ensures that your narrative flows logically from one point to the next.

Saves Time: With a solid outline, you can avoid meandering through unnecessary tangents. Knowing what to write next saves time and keeps you focused on moving forward with your manuscript.

Facilitates Research: An outline helps you identify areas that require research, allowing you to gather all the necessary information before you begin writing. This means you can write more efficiently because you have all your resources at hand.

Helps Maintain Consistency: For both fiction and non-fiction, consistency in tone, style, and content is key. An outline keeps you on track, ensuring that each chapter aligns with the overall theme and objectives of your book.

Allows for Flexibility: While it might seem counterintuitive, a strong outline actually provides the flexibility to make changes. With a clear framework in place, you can shift sections around without losing sight of the big picture.

Reduces Overwhelm: Writing a book can be an overwhelming task. An outline breaks down the process into manageable pieces, making it less daunting and helping you to stay motivated.

One tendency of AI, particularly in the early days was to hallucinate - boldly stating ‘facts’ where no such facts existed. Thankfully, this is becoming less of a problem as the technology matures.

When an AI "hallucinates," it refers to the AI generating incorrect, nonsensical, or completely fabricated information that is not based on the data it has been trained on or the input it has received.


This can happen for several reasons:

Out-of-Domain Queries: If the AI is asked about topics or in a format it wasn't properly trained to understand, it may generate irrelevant or nonsensical responses.


Insufficient Training Data: The AI may not have been exposed to enough varied examples during its training to accurately generate responses for certain prompts.


Complex or Ambiguous Prompts: When prompts are too vague or overly complex, the AI might struggle to discern the intended meaning and produce a coherent response.


Bias in Training Data: If the training data contained biases or misinformation, the AI might replicate these in its responses.


Overfitting or Underfitting: Overfitting occurs when an AI is too closely tied to the specifics of its training data and can't generalize well, while underfitting happens when it hasn't learned enough from its training data to make accurate predictions.


Model Limitations: Every AI model has limitations in terms of what it can understand and how it can respond. Pushing these boundaries can lead to hallucination.


When an AI hallucinates, it's important to recognize that its output is not reliable and should be verified against trusted sources or corrected through further training and refinement of the AI model.

Building a Cohesive Structure

The organization of chapters and sections in non-fiction writing is akin to constructing a building: each chapter is a floor, each section a room, and together they form a structure that is both functional and informative.

A well-organized structure enables readers to navigate through the content logically and intuitively, understanding how each part contributes to the whole. It is the skeleton that supports the flesh of your argument, allowing it to stand upright and be examined from all angles.


Laying the Foundation: Chapter Outline

Start with a solid foundation by outlining each chapter. This outline should encapsulate the main points and subpoints you plan to cover. Think of it as a blueprint that sketches out the boundaries and connections between the ideas you will present.

Define the Purpose: Each chapter should have a clear purpose that ties back to your thesis. This purpose will guide the content and organization of the chapter.

Identify Key Points: Determine the key points that will support the chapter's purpose. These will become your section headings.

Logical Sequence: Arrange the key points in a logical sequence that builds your argument or narrative in a coherent manner.

Transitions: Plan for transitions that will connect each point smoothly, aiding the reader's journey from one concept to the next.


Constructing the Framework: Section Organization

With the chapter outlines in place, focus on structuring the sections. Each section should delve into a specific aspect of the chapter's theme, providing evidence, examples, and analysis.

Topic Sentences: Begin each section with a topic sentence that introduces the main idea. This sentence acts as a signpost for the reader, indicating what to expect.

Consistent Subheadings: Use consistent subheadings to break up the text and make it easier to follow. Subheadings act as minor signposts, guiding the reader through the section.

Concluding Sentences: End each section with a concluding sentence or a transition that leads to the next point. This helps reinforce the main idea and sets up the next section.


Erecting the Structure: Chapter Development

As you fill in the content for each chapter, ensure that the development of ideas maintains a steady pace that is neither too rushed nor too drawn out.

Balance: Strike to maintain balance in the depth and length of each chapter. Aim for uniformity in how much time you spend on each concept, unless certain sections require more emphasis due to their complexity or importance.

Foreshadowing: At the end of a chapter, foreshadow what's coming next to pique the reader's interest and provide a logical progression.

Recapitulation: At the beginning of a new chapter, briefly recap the main points of the previous one to refresh the reader's memory and establish connections.

Review and Revision: Ensuring Coherence

Use AI for Compelling Dialogue

Dialogue is the lifeblood of any story, conveying character, advancing plot, and adding pace. Writing compelling dialogue with AI involves understanding speech patterns, character voice, and the subtleties of human conversation.

Developing effective dialogue is an art that requires attention to detail, an understanding of character, and an ear for authenticity.

Here are three key principles to keep in mind when crafting dialogue:

Character Voice and Distinctiveness: Each character should have a unique voice that reflects their background, personality, and current emotional state. This distinctiveness helps readers differentiate between characters and adds depth to their portrayals.

To achieve this, consider each character's education level, cultural background, personal experiences, and their role in the story. A teenager will speak differently from an elderly professor; a street-smart detective will use different vernacular than a tech entrepreneur.

Their dialogue should also reflect their emotional and psychological states, providing insight into their motivations and reactions to various situations.

Subtext and Realism: Good dialogue often says more than the words spoken aloud. Subtext—the underlying meaning or intention behind the words—can reveal the true emotions and thoughts of a character.

Realistic dialogue often involves characters not saying what they mean or saying one thing and meaning another. This can create tension, humor, or drama, and makes the interaction more engaging.

To write dialogue with effective subtext, understand what each character wants in a scene and how they would (or wouldn't) express that desire. Realism in dialogue also means avoiding exposition dumps where characters tell each other information they already know for the sake of informing the audience; instead, find natural ways to reveal necessary information.

Purpose and Pacing: Every piece of dialogue should serve a purpose within the story. It could be to advance the plot, reveal character traits, increase tension, provide comic relief, or foreshadow future events.

Avoid filler words that do not add value to the conversation or the story. Pacing is also crucial; the rhythm of a conversation can influence the mood of a scene.

Short, quick exchanges might create a sense of urgency or tension, while longer monologues might be used for introspection or to provide a break in the action. Pay attention to the flow of dialogue and consider how it affects the overall pacing of the narrative.

By focusing on these three aspects — character voice and distinctiveness, subtext and realism, and purpose and pacing — writers can craft dialogue that resonates with readers and brings their characters to life.

My Personal Strategy: Pre-Writing, Free Writing, and Rewriting

In the journey of crafting a book, authors often navigate through a series of creative stages that serve to refine and enhance their narrative.

When my writing is flowing best, the process typically unfolds in three pivotal phases: pre-writing, free-writing, and rewriting. Each stage plays a crucial role in the development of a well-rounded and compelling article, book or story.

Pre-writing: This initial phase is all about laying the groundwork for your book. It involves brainstorming ideas, conducting research, outlining plot points, and developing characters.

Pre-writing is the strategic planning of your argument or narrative, where you decide the direction and scope of your story. It's akin to an architect drafting blueprints before construction begins; it sets the foundation for everything that follows.

Free writing: Here, you let your creativity flow without inhibition. Free writing is about getting your ideas down on paper without worrying about grammar, style, or coherence.

It's a liberating phase where you can explore different paths your story might take, flesh out scenes, and dive deeper into your characters' psyches. This stage is essential for unlocking new insights and overcoming writer's block.

Rewriting: In this final stage, you refine your manuscript. Rewriting involves editing for structure, style, and clarity, ensuring consistency in tone and pacing, and polishing dialogue and descriptions.

It's a meticulous process of revision that transforms your rough draft into a polished piece worthy of publication.

Why This Process Works:

The pre-writing, free-writing, and rewriting process works because it mirrors the natural progression of thought and creativity. It allows you to plan, explore, and then refine, ensuring that each aspect of your story receives the attention it deserves.

This is How I Became a Best-Selling Author

My first book was published by McGraw Hill in the early 1990s and is still available on Amazon to this day.

After making some money trading the futures market and paying for a family trip to Australia, I decided to write a book about using technical analysis to trade the financial markets.  My book is titled “How to Make Money Trading Stocks and Commodities: Finally…a Sensible Approach for the Intelligent Investor.”

I wrote this book in the old days when getting published by a recognized publisher was an extremely difficult and challenging pursuit.  I self-published the first edition of my book in 1987; back when nearly all books were printed on paper.  Digital books were few and far between.

I  navigated the full journey of book creation, from the writing and editing phases to collaborating with a graphic designer for the interior layout and visuals. I personally designed the cover and delivered the print-ready digital files to the commercial printer. I ordered 2,000  copies.

One day a delivery truck pulled up to my house filled with boxes of books. Holding your first book is surreal.

Once the books were safely stashed in my garage, the next step was to tackle the toughest part of the whole exercise: marketing and distribution. 

I contacted the nearby bookstores, and they were all very responsive and excellent to work with.  Every store agreed to stock several copies of my book in the interests of supporting a local author. 

Once these copies were sold, I knew that my book had potential.

I reached out to one of the few book distributors working with self-published authors at the time: Gordon Soules Book Publishers Ltd based out of Vancouver.  After a fine lunch meeting, Mr. Soules signed me up and I sent him a couple of boxes of books.  Bingo... my books began to appear in bookstores across Canada! 

He suggested a book tour to Vancouver and Toronto and organized radio interviews that were broadcast across the country. This was a great help in marketing my book.  A book tour was much more important in the days before social media!

I still remember the feeling of walking into the biggest bookstore in the nation and spotting several copies of my book staring back at me.  I even remembered an old trick an experienced author had taught me; “When visiting a bookstore,” he said, “never leave your books facing spine out.” 

Of course, bookstores like to save space by cramming as many books as possible onto their shelves. Normally, only big-name authors get their books displayed with the cover facing out. By the time I left the bookstore my books were nicely arranged…face out.

After selling most of my stock of 2,000 books, I contacted an agent to see if a mainstream publisher might be interested in publishing my book.  Within less than a week she responded with the news that McGraw Hill was interested. 

This seemed like winning the lottery!  McGraw Hill was, and is, one of the most renowned publishers in North America.

My agent successfully negotiated a respectable advance on royalties and my book, sporting a professionally designed cover, hit bookstore shelves across the nation.

My book continued to sell well.  According to Friesen Press, “a book that sells around 5,000 copies in Canada should qualify for bestseller status.” 

My book has sold over 8,000 copies and is still selling today.
