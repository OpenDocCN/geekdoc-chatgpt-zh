Introduction

ChatGPT simplifies access to powerful AI technology, making it available to almost anyone. This book embraces a “Start with Simplicity” approach to help you leverage ChatGPT effectively.

Think of ChatGPT as an AI appliance. Like any appliance, it requires some basic understanding to use effectively. While ChatGPT is not an Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), it can solve specific problems with impressive efficiency.

The key to using ChatGPT successfully lies in adopting a “Black-box Mindset”. This approach allows you to focus on the inputs and outputs you can control, without needing to understand the complex inner workings of the AI. By concentrating on what you can control, you can achieve better results and improve your skills over time.

In this book, you’ll learn how to:

  • Approach ChatGPT with a black box mindset.
  • Start with simple techniques and add complexity as needed.
  • Identify the best problems for ChatGPT to solve.

Our goal is to help you use ChatGPT effectively without getting lost in AI jargon. Let’s begin your journey to mastering ChatGPT with simplicity.

Artificial Intelligence: The New Generic Application

“AI is the new electricity”—Andrew Ng, AI Researcher, Entrepreneur and Thought Leader

Andrew Ng argues that Artificial Intelligence is a general-purpose technology, meaning that it is not useful for just one thing, but useful for many things.

In this chapter I want to give you a flavour of this powerful capability and propose a different way that we should think of applications like ChatGPT.

I argue that the analogy for ChatGPT is an appliance that uses the AI electricity.

Start with simplicity

The proposition of this book is that successfully using ChatGPT is an exercise in uncovering the practical simplicity in our interactions with ChatGPT. If you read articles about ChatGPT online you’ll be bombarded with multiple messages that obscure this practical simplicity. Either the statements are over-simplistic in making claims about what ChatGPT can do by leaving out critical and non-trivial integration concerns. Or the messages are incredibly complex and dive deep into the internals of the GPT AI engine.

I believe that practical use of ChatGPT can avoid the latter: you don’t really need to know much about GPT internals. First, because you don’t need to know them to make effective use of the tool and second because much of the information you read is misleading, wrong or poorly explained.

On the other hand, you do need to know just the right about about the practical integration of ChatGPT into your work processes. What I mean by “just the right amount” is that the information must be balanced against the simplicity or complexity of your use cases. In the early stages of your ChatGPT experience, you need to know no more than is related to the tools and data that you use.

The Artificial Intelligence “Tipping Point”

Artificial Intelligence has been around for decades but has sped up out of sight these short months since OpenAI released ChatGPT.

Use of ChatGPT and its competitors is exploding. And so, it seems, is its misuse. People are flocking to use it based on vague claims, assumptions, prejudices, and false expectations. Whilst many people are surprised and pleased by the value that ChatGPT helps them create, others find their expectations are not met.

The release of ChatGPT in November 2022 was a “tipping point” in AI enablement, after which consumers’ engagement with AI capabilities changes materially. The ChatGPT browser-based interface made AI technology available to anyone interested and attracted enormous interest.

“And the more you look, the more you see what has changed with this model — and why it seems like a tipping point. … ChatGPT, now open to everyone, has made an important transition.”—Ethan Mollick in “ChatGPT Is a Tipping Point for AI” (hbr.org 14th December 2022).

What is the Tipping Point?

The Tipping Point Unpacked

Prior to the “Tipping Point” the use of AI in any business or technology are was limited by at least one (and usually two) non-trivial enablers:

  1. Development of an AI software solution
  2. Training of the AI model

At the “Tipping Point” of AI these factors were turn on their heads.

Cost of Implementation

The cost of AI solutions includes many factors, for example:

  1. Developer cost to design, code, and test the solution. This varies with the platform and integration environment, which progressively have provided more built-in capabilities and therefore less custom code.
  2. Cost of the platform: some are open source and some proprietary, but as we all know “open source” doesn’t mean “no cost”.
  3. Infrastructure cost: the non-trivial cost of computing infrastructure, whether physical or virtualised.
  4. Time to market: time is a cost, not the least of which includes the overhead burden that someone must carry while the solution is developed. There can also be huge costs if market windows are missed.
  5. Model training: the cost of training the model: data licence costs, infrastructure costs, and people costs are the big three.

Model Training

All Machine Learning (“ML”) solutions require training on data that covers the intended use of the solution.

Before the “Tipping Point”, solution developers or specialised customer personnel trained AI models on the data that was directly related to the use case an organisation was trying to solve with the AI solution.

If the AI integration platform chosen was a product, the development process would have involved conducting model training. However, whether the situation permitted the trained models to be made available or not relied on the specific circumstances.

What OpenAI and other GenAI platform vendors did was to train their models on increasingly massive amounts of data from a broad range of sources without any specific application use case in mind. Or, if they perhaps had reference use cases in mind, it was a very broad set.

This model “pre-training” involves training of the model the vendor performs on the AI platform before release. For example, OpenAI say they trained GPT on all the Wikipedia data, which includes millions of pages and hundreds of thousands of categories and links.

The result of the massive pre-training is that the product is available for immediate use by anyone, at least in theory. Andrew Ng provided an interesting quantification of this below.

ChatGPT: The AI Appliance for Everyone

OpenAI’s ChatGPT has dramatically increased public access to artificial intelligence (AI). It simplifies AI interaction, offering both opportunities and challenges, and can be thought of as an AI appliance accessible to anyone via the internet.

The Complexity and Opacity of GPT

GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) is a sophisticated and complex machine. While some aspects of its architecture and functionality are transparent, a significant portion remains proprietary and opaque.

Many existing articles and papers that try to explain ChatGPT either become too technical or rely on superficial explanations. These pieces often end up confusing the user rather than clarifying how ChatGPT works. Even when experts do attempt to unpack the technology, like Stephen Wolfram’s detailed analysis, the depth and complexity make it difficult for the average user to grasp.

There’s a high level of misunderstanding about what ChatGPT can and cannot do. This is partly because explanations often mix up answers to multiple questions, like “How does ChatGPT work?”, “How was it built?”, and “What is Artificial Intelligence?”. Marketing factors also contribute to this confusion.

Just like a household appliance, ChatGPT has a simplified user interface and specific functionalities. It’s designed for a broad user base and hides its internal complexities.

Characteristics of an Appliance

  1. Specific Functional Profile: It does what it claims to do.
  2. Simplified User Interface: Only essential controls are exposed.
  3. Hidden Internal Technology: What’s “under the hood” remains largely concealed.
  4. Target Audience: Aimed at the public, not just tech-savvy users.

However, ChatGPT is not a perfect appliance because of its probabilistic nature and sometimes incorrect responses. Despite the marketing and user interfaces simplifying the experience, the range of its functionalities remains limited.

Knowing the inner workings of ChatGPT is mostly irrelevant for users. What matters more is understanding what it does well and how to maximise its utility for your specific needs.

Crossing the Skills Bootstrapping Chasm

We all start out as novices at anything we do. And when we start out, we all “suck” at it. But with the proper practice and help, we get better. Kathy Sierra describes this as starting in the “Suck Zone”. I’ve extended this idea to ChatGPT and call this process “Skills Bootstrapping”, because much of what we learn with ChatGPT emerges from the interaction cycle. We use the experience of recent interactions to bootstrap our skills for upcoming cycles. The problem is that without direction it can be a random activity.

You can see that Kathy loosely divides ability (aka expertise) into three zones:

  1. “Suck” Zone: beginners and others who generally “suck” at the skill.
  2. “Competent” Zone: people who have overcome the “Suck Threshold” and become confident and productive.
  3. “Badass” Zone: someone highly proficient — “world-class” at this skill.

“Along the way they’ll hit many milestones, some more crucial than others. But of all points on the curve, the two most important are crossing the Suck Threshold and crossing the Badass Threshold.” in “Badass: Making Users Awesome” (p. 67). O’Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.

The emphasis here is on the word “right”. Using the tool won’t help you escape what Kathy Sierra calls “The Suck Zone”. Try different learning strategies to make the progress shown in the above diagram.

If we don’t have some help with our approach to progressive development of ChatGPT skills, then we risk being blocked by the “chasm” - the vast gap between very basic skills and getting out of the “suck zone”.

The Skills Boostrapping Chasm gap is a transitional blocker that most people experience: we have just enough skills to make ChatGPT “work” - to input prompts and get results, but we don’t have enough skills to prevent poor use or even mis-use. In other words we know enough to get ourselves into trouble but not enough to get out of trouble.

This chasm is caused as much by frustration as by lack of skill. This is particularly the case if people try to get too far ahead with their knowledge of ChatGPT internals or by attempting to learn Prompt Engineering too soon. Another way is to buy lists of prompts: it’s fine if they work but if they need tweaking to work in your environment or particular use case, perhaps you don’t have the skill to make the change effectively.

The result of not “Crossing the Chasm” is being stuck in the “Suck Zone” because the frustration and poor results outweigh the time and effort you’re prepared to put in.

The way across the skills bootstrapping chasm is by getting help that is targeted and appropriate to the precise stage and circumstances of each user. This includes such things as coaches or mentors, high-quality courses, and, of course, high-quality books like this one.

The ChatGPT Black Box Mindset

A “black box” is a system whose internal operations are not understood by the user. Users only interact with it through its inputs and outputs.

Adopting a “black box” mindset with ChatGPT has several advantages. It frees us from misconceptions, allows us to focus on what we can control, and facilitates a better understanding of ChatGPT’s strengths and weaknesses.

This is a very simple concept, but it can be a double-edged sword, introducing downsides as well as upsides:

  1. It opens a market for people wanting to explain the internals, even if they themselves don’t fully understand it.
  2. It leads to user-generated myths and misunderstandings about what’s happening inside the box.
  3. It opens up the potential for “Cargo Cult AI,” where people form incorrect theories about what’s happening inside the box.

The first step to use ChatGPT effectively is to accept its complexity as a unit and embrace it as a black box. This enables us to focus on the inputs and outputs, which are within our control.

A substantial part of the current market offerings for ChatGPT revolves around prompts. However, these often ignore guidelines about evaluating the outputs or understanding the variations in responses due to ChatGPT’s probabilistic nature.

Understanding how to better control inputs and evaluate outputs is crucial for optimising ChatGPT’s utility. It’s not just about making the process seem “plug-and-play.”

While you can pursue a deeper understanding of how GPT, AI, and ML work, such knowledge isn’t necessary for the practical use of ChatGPT as an AI appliance. A “black box” approach can often be more beneficial, helping you focus on what you can control: the inputs and the outputs.

Unpacking the ChatGPT Black Box Mindset

We can describe the Black Box Mindset to the ChatGPT AI Appliance as comprising six factors.

Six Components Enable One Mindset

The Black Box ChatGPT Interaction Framework guides users in effectively leveraging ChatGPT’s capabilities while addressing inherent limitations and ensuring responsible usage within organisations. This framework encompasses six key components that collectively enable users to interact with ChatGPT in a productive, ethical and informed manner. The six components are: Engage the Black-Box Interface, Play to ChatGPT Strengths, Understand Critical ChatGPT Constraints, Focus on What the User Can Control, Adopt Curious Abundance, and Implement Black-Box Governance. The figure below summarises these components.

1. Engage the Black-Box Interface

The “GPT Black-box Interface” is the GPT equivalent of the Turing Test, which I wrote about in GPT Doctrine #2. Your interaction with GPT is based only on your inputs and GPT’s outputs. You ignore (or perhaps suppress) what happens on the other side of the interface. It could be a person, a troop of monkeys, or an alien mother-ship. You don’t care. You care about what you get as output for its use and to inform your future inputs. The Black-Box interface is the ultimate in empiricism.

2. Play to ChatGPT Strengths

To harness ChatGPT’s full potential, users must play to its strengths. ChatGPT excels in natural language processing, human-like interactions, and self-improving capabilities. By focusing on these strengths, users can effectively use the model for various applications, such as content creation, customer support, and data analysis. Recognising and leveraging these strengths ensures that interactions with ChatGPT are both efficient and effective. ChatGPT’s strengths are as amazing as its weaknesses are surprising. We must not mistake a strength at a superficial level as being an indication of underlying capability that just doesn’t exist.

3. Understand Critical ChatGPT Constraints

Just was we must be aware of GPT’s strengths and not kid ourselves of their origins, we must also be objective and mindful of GPT’s significant weaknesses. I call them ‘constraints’ because they are indicators of where not to use ChatGPT - guidelines to avoid certain kins of problems lest we get blocked with substandard results. In the “Black-box Mindset”, we understand four critical sets of constraints that you must keep in mind when structuring your use of GPT. These constraints are 1) No Sentient Knowledge, 2) Non-deterministic responses, 3) Response Limitations and 4) Errors in Plain Sight (e.g. Bias and Non-explainability). (I’ll expand on these in a later yarn). More recently, we can probably add a fifth constraint: infrastructure performance and availability. Since DevDay in 2023, the OpenAI platform has been highly variable in its performance. (That’s a neutral way of saying, some days it sucks!) One day, I’ll ask GPT to process a mass of data and get almost an instant response. On other days, it’s unusable on even simple tasks.

4. Focus on What the User Can Control

There are three factors that you, as a user, have complete control over when using GPT. These are 1) Problem Selection, 2) Prompt Structure and Content and 3) How you use the responses. Everything else is built into the GPT architecture or under the control of OpenAI or, more recently, other software partners such as plugin builders and GPTs developers. The “Black-box Mindset” helps you understand the three things you have control over, and leverage them for your best outcomes.

5. Adopt Curious Abundance

The “Black-box Mindset” includes a curious questioning openness to the fact that GPT is constantly evolving and that we don’t always know everything about what it does or how it does it. “Curious Abundance” underscores the almost magical nature of GPT: we can never say with any certainty that we know everything about its capabilities. Not only is OpenAI continually tweaking the models and developing new capabilities, but there is evidence of a “Ghost in the Machine” that surprises us with things we never expected.

6. Implement Black-Box Governance

Where GPT is used in enterprise environments of any sort, we need to consider some level of governance. Governance is — broadly speaking — the set of policies, procedures, and tools that help organisations protect themselves from negative events, such as security breaches. The term “Black-box Governance” is almost a contradiction because governance is ultimately about risk management (RM), and with RM, you want to know as much about the risk parameters as possible. But we play with the cards OpenAI deals us. Some things are improving, but some things, like “Explainability”, are unlikely ever to be addressed in GPT because of architectural constraints.

Conclusion

The Black Box ChatGPT Interaction Framework provides a comprehensive approach to engaging with ChatGPT. By addressing each of the six key components, users and organisations can enhance the effectiveness, reliability, and ethical use of ChatGPT. This framework empowers users to navigate the complexities of interacting with a black-box AI model, ensuring their use of ChatGPT is innovative and responsible.

The Human-ChatGPT Black-box Interface

The “Black-box interface” is a concept that simplifies Human-ChatGPT interaction by focusing on inputs and outputs while disregarding the GPT engine’s complex internals. This empirical approach helps users leverage ChatGPT’s strengths effectively and objectively. The Black-box Interface is the ChatGPT extension of the “Imitation Game” conceived by Alan Turing.

In the original “Imitation Game”, also called the “Turing Test”, the concept was for a person (C) to interact with something on the other side of a wall, by sending written messages back and forth through a slot. The challenge was for the person (C) to determine whether another person (B) was on the other side of the wall, or it was a computer (A).

The Black-box interface is the natural extension of this, because we don’t care what’s on the other side of the wall. Our purpose is to solve problems or perform other functions based on the responses we receive.

Engaging with ChatGPT via the Black-box interface involves understanding the model’s operational dynamics without necessarily knowing its internal workings.

So, we end up with the simplest of interaction concepts for ChatGPT, one which ignores what goes on “inside the box”.

ChatGPT Characteristics

Let’s review the key characteristics of ChatGPT that sit behind the fluff and hyperbole. What really makes it tick?

ChatGPT is just a SaaS

On the other side of the ChatGPT interface is just a computer program, delivered in a “Software as a Service” model. Sure it’s powerful, but it’s just a SaaS: it has features and feature gaps; it has distinct strengths and weaknesses. The platform has bugs and outages. Its developers and operators are humans who mostly do awesome things but also make mistakes. We’ve lived with SaaS products for decades, and ChatGPT is not much different in that respect. In some ways, it is potentially more vulnerable than other because it has grown so fast.

SaaS is Incredibly Powerful and Complex and New

The GPT technology stack is enormously complex system that took many months and gazillions of dollars to build. Only a few people in the world truly understand how it works, although many think they do—a sort of LLM Dunning-Kruger effect. Many of the ideas and opinions about ChatGPT capabilities are based on Artificial Intelligence and computing concepts from before the AI “Tipping Point” and so are potentially incorrect, misleading or flat-out unhelpful.

ChatGPT SaaS provides almost Zero guidance on use

The user interface to the ChatGPT client is beautifully simple—it’s just a text box into which we type natural language. There’s no real vendor guidance on how to use it, just a bunch of highly technical descriptions of the internals and a limited description of capabilities. There are gazillions of information sources all over the world. These sources of information often lack accuracy and quality production, with many full of hype and unvalidated assertions. It seems there is an assumption that using natural language for an interface requires less guidance – folks will just “figure it out”.

We’re Seduced by Natural Language

By tapping into natural language, ChatGPT taps into something primal in our brains, which probably makes ChatGPT seem far more intelligent and capable than it is. How humans structure language maps directly into how we think (or at least how we think we think). Society has conditioned us to associate excellent language use with intelligence and other positive attributes. It’s difficult to avoid this reaction when we encounter machine responses even moderately eloquent. Language is also culture, so ChatGPT also taps into our cultural sense of belonging. Through the magic of natural language, ChatGPT both persuades us that it is highly intelligent and taps into our anthropomorphic tendencies.

Conclusion

ChatGPT is no doubt extremely powerful, but its design sings us a siren song towards misunderstanding the product and misapplication of our efforts. Engaging with ChatGPT via the Black-box interface will help us avoid the siren call.

GPT Key Strengths

Natural Language Processing

GPT-4 exhibits a remarkable understanding of human language. It can generate human-like text that is contextually relevant, grammatically correct, and coherent over long passages. This makes it a powerful tool for a variety of tasks, such as drafting project documentation, generating code, and facilitating communication among stakeholders.

Natural Language Processing includes four key functions: Prompt Language Parsing, Separation of Concerns, Content Completion & Transformation, and Output Language Moderation.

Human-like Interactions

GPT models can simulate interactions that are coherent, contextually relevant, and personalised. The key aspects include the ability to interact with Plausible conversations, Personalised responses, and Real-time generation.

Plausible: GPT models generate responses that are plausible and contextually appropriate.

Personalised: Personalisation is a critical aspect of human-like interactions. GPT models can tailor responses based on user preferences, previous interactions, and specific contextual information.

I’m not going to go into detail on the OpenAI Playground here. But the parameters are illustrated in the following diagram and described in more detail after that.

There are two areas you can add messages that are submitted to ChatGPT for processing (“completion”).

  1. User Message: The input text that you provide to the model. (also called a “prompt”)
  2. System Message: An additional text input that is submitted to the model with the prompt to give additional context.

There are eight (8) parameters you can set that control how the GPT engine processes the prompts and generates an answer:

  1. Mode: this is a legacy parameter – leave it set to “Chat” unless you know what you’re doing
  2. Model: Specifies which GPT model to use (e.g., `text-davinci-002`, `text-ada-002`).
  3. Temperature: Controls the randomness of the model’s output (Range: 0 to 1).
  4. Maximum Length: Limits the number of tokens in the model’s output. (also called “max_tokens”)
  5. Stop Sequences: Character strings that will stop generating further tokens. The returned text will not contain the stop sequence. You can enter up to 4 Stop sequences.
  6. TopP: Filters the model’s vocabulary before sampling the next token. (Range: 0 to 1) (also called “top_p “)
  7. Frequency Penalty: Penalises or rewards frequently occurring tokens. (Range: -2 to 2)
  8. Presence Penalty: Penalises or rewards new tokens. (Range: -2 to 2)

  • Save Button: a button to save a named version of the current configuration.
  • View Code Button: a button that shows a code version of the current configuration that can be copied and pasted into a code editor.
  • Share Button: allows you to share an existing configuration with others.

Audit Tracking: Implementing audit trails for interactions with ChatGPT is essential. Other: Additional audit responsibilities may include regular reviews of ChatGPT usage.

Disclosure and Citation

Transparency in the use of ChatGPT is critical. This involves full disclosure of AI involvement in content creation and proper citation of sources and contributions.

Full Disclosure: Organisations should disclose when and how ChatGPT is used, particularly in content creation. How to Explain: Providing clear explanations about the capabilities and limitations of ChatGPT is essential. How to Cite: Establishing guidelines for citing ChatGPT’s contributions ensures that the use of AI-generated content is properly acknowledged and traceable.

Quality and Use of Results

Ensuring the quality and appropriate use of ChatGPT’s outputs involves verification, validation, and addressing potential risks such as injection and errors.

Verification: Organisations should verify the accuracy and reliability of ChatGPT’s responses before using them in decision-making processes. Validation: Validation processes should be in place to assess the relevance and applicability of ChatGPT’s outputs. Injection: Organisations need to be aware of and mitigate risks related to data injection attacks. Errors: Establishing protocols for identifying and correcting errors in ChatGPT’s responses is crucial.

Conclusion

Black Box Governance is a comprehensive framework for managing the use of ChatGPT in organisations. By focusing on knowledge of suppliers, audit responsibilities, disclosure and citation, and the quality and use of results, organisations can ensure that their use of ChatGPT is transparent, ethical, and aligned with their strategic objectives. This approach enhances the trust and reliability of AI applications and empowers organisations to leverage ChatGPT’s capabilities responsibly and effectively.

Organic prompting

This section summarises an approach to ChatGPT interaction I call “Organic Prompting.” This approach aligns with the strategic mindset of focusing on outcomes and impacts rather than techniques and deliverables.

In keeping with the primary proposition of this book, Organic Prompting starts with simplicity and the question “what can I remove to achieve perfection?”

Take what works for you and ignore the rest.

But, I can already hear people asking: “What about Prompt Engineering?”. So, let’s address this first.

Why Not Just Start with Prompt Engineering?

I assume that you’ve read articles or discussions on ChatGPT. In that case, you’ve probably seen references to “Prompt Engineering,” a loosely defined practice that defines rules and techniques for creating prompts for artificial intelligence “chatbots” like ChatGPT.

I have nothing against Prompt Engineering as a body of knowledge. Personally, I’m very interested in the various prompt types and the reason that they work.

“Prompt Engineering” emerged out of the long history of natural language interfaces to older AI systems. There is much to be gained from Prompt Engineering, but it is not a formal practice, and the term “Engineering” is a bit of overkill. In my long experience in the information technology field, I’ve seen many emerging practices have the word “engineering” slapped on them to give the impression of something formalised, organised, and unified.

The intent is there, but I’m always concerned with recommendations that focus on the “what” and “how” of something rather than the “why”.

If we focus too much on techniques in the prompt crafting for language models like ChatGPT, we can forget the reasons we’re doing this in the first place.

The whole point of the “Black-box Mindset” is to nudge people to think about what they are trying to achieve by using ChatGPT and less about how it works. Although prompting is the external interface between the world and ChatGPT, prompt engineering exposes users to the internal operation and capabilities.

It’s very common for users to micro-manage ChatGPT with overly technical and prescriptive prompts. By over-specifying the prompt, users inadvertently constrain the model’s potential to generate creative and effective outputs.

You should assume that everything you put into a prompt is used by ChatGPT as content when generating its responses – see section “A Shallow Dive Into GPT Functionality”.

Some circumstances undoubtedly require more complex prompts. But start simple and scale up as needed.

The Foundations of Organic Prompting

The foundation of “Organic Prompting” for ChatGPT is simplifying the interaction between the user and the tool. It focuses on clear, context-driven prompts that capture the intended outcome rather than the specifics of the process.

User Accessibility: Organic Prompting lowers the barrier to entry for casual or non-technical users who may not be adept at constructing complex prompts (yet).

Efficiency: By focusing on outcomes, users can quickly and effectively communicate their needs, potentially leading to faster and more relevant responses.

Quality of Results: It encourages the LLM to generate responses aligned with the user’s end goals, which may improve the output’s usability and applicability.

Encourages Strategic Thinking: Organic Prompting encourages users to think beyond the mere immediate generation of the response and more about the strategic use of the information provided.

Flexibility: Organic Prompting allows AI more creative freedom to draw upon its extensive training data to provide answers, often resulting in more innovative and comprehensive solutions. A good rule of design is that a good design is easier to change than a bad one, which applies to prompts as much as anything.

Scalability: Organic Prompting allows users to start at the most simple stage and progress smoothly as their requirements and circumstances require. Simple things are easy, but hard things are possible. There has been a natural progression from organic prompting to other prompt frameworks and, ultimately, prompt engineering.

Principles of Organic Prompting

Organic Prompting is based on the premise that prompts don’t need complexity; they need clarity and context. Prompts should be actionable. Organic Prompting believes that the user is the only judge of the usefulness of ChatGPT’s responses.

Emergence Rocks

Get started! Then, see what happens. The best prompts emerge from iterative interactions. Avoid thinking you need to “get everything right in one go”. Run everything through the model and observe the outcomes.

Acceptability is the only Quality

The only measure of quality is whether the results from ChatGPT are acceptable to you in your specific problem and situation.

You can’t always get what you want, but sometimes you get what you need.

Simplicity Rules (and always has)

The art of maximising the amount of work not done is essential. Clear prompts are the cornerstone of effective communication with ChatGPT. Ask yourself, “Can I remove anything and still get the same result or better?”

Natural Language is Best

Describe what you want as naturally as possible. Communicate with ChatGPT using straightforward language but don’t agonise over it or spend time writing multiple drafts outside of ChatGPT. Let the model do the magic! Write your prompts as you would explain something to a colleague or family member.

Enablement Guides Process

Describe what you want to enable through the interaction. After interacting with ChatGPT and receiving its response, what do you want to be able to do that you couldn’t do before? Think of active words like “understand,” “communicate,” “analyse,” and “apply.” See how to use this in the breakout below.

Don’t describe the steps or how ChatGPT should solve the problem unless you find that you can’t get an acceptable result without getting to that level of detail.

Context Is Key

Providing context allows ChatGPT to understand the prompt’s intention. Context frames the problem space without dictating specific methods for the solution.

One element of context is the role that you want ChatGPT to play.

“By using ChatGPT I will be able to …. in a topic or problem area.”

“With the information generated by ChatGPT I will be able to …. in a topic or problem area.”

Understand: Know a topic’s key concepts and principles and articulate similarities, differences, and challenges.

Apply: implement practical techniques and tools.

Analyse: assess the situation critically and produce a summary or root cause, or ask additional clarifying questions**.**

Evaluate: assess the effectiveness and utility of different options and make a choice or decision.

Create: design and write a new work such as an article, artwork, plan, or data table.

Remember: recall and reference essential principles, best practices, facts, and ideas.

Example of Organic Prompting

Say you’re writing a master’s dissertation and want some information on a particular topic.

You could get into very specific detail which projects your concerns

“Write 500 words on the topic of how current legislation affects small-scale solar power adoption. Write clearly and succinctly and avoid AI detection. Include citations for the information and include a number of quotes. Include a “References” list that includes the full details of citations in APA 7th edition format.”

This form of prompt is very common and originates in the user’s mind that they have to specify how ChatGPT should go about doing a task. The user is taking their knowledge of a problem domain and projecting into ChatGPT the set of rules and constraints that they feel are important. I’m sure this user will get some results. Try pasting this into ChatGPT and see what you get.

Here’s an alternative approach, following the principles of Organic Prompting. Instead of giving ChatGPT specific instructions like “avoid AI detection,” reframe your prompt by stating the desired outcome.

“I’m writing an academic paper on renewable energy policies for my master’s dissertation at the University of [name]. I need insights on how current legislation affects small-scale solar power adoption.”

This is the simplest prompt you could imagine. It clearly defines the purpose, context, and intended use of the information, allowing ChatGPT to generate a response that is aligned with your needs. Naming the university opens up the possibility that ChatGPT can use any information from the institution (or similar institutions) that relates to master’s dissertations, including plagiarism and referencing requirements.

If you get the results you need, you’re done and moving on to the next task. If you don’t get the result you want, then you can expand this a little to the following

“I’m writing an academic paper on renewable energy policies for my master’s dissertation at the University of [name]. I need insights on how current legislation affects small-scale solar power adoption. Include source citations and a reference list. Target the information so that a scholarly audience will find enough detail, and the response will comply with generally accepted academic plagiarism prohibitions.”

In some cases you’ll use the full prompt and in others you’ll break it up due to the evolutionary nature of the prompt-response cycle.

In the context of Organic Prompting, a nudge is a subtle suggestion embedded within a prompt to guide ChatGPT toward a preferred outcome. Much like nudges in behavioral science, these phrases influence the AI’s behavior in a predictable way without restricting its options or forcing a specific response. Nudges gently steer the AI, making it more likely to provide a useful answer while maintaining the flexibility and creativity of its responses.

Here are a few high-impact statements that you can include in your Organic Prompts as nudges to influence the interaction flow or results.

Controlling flow

This statement stops ChatGPT responding before you’re ready. Often I will provide a number of input prompts before I ask a question or set a task. I usually define the relationship and job roles in one prompt. Then provide input data in one or more prompts, and then I’ll ask a series of questions. “please don’t respond until I give you a specific task or ask a specific question”

“please generate your response in a code window so I can easily copy the results to other applications”

I see numerous statements in prompts almost every day that people add because they think it will “tell” ChatGPT to behave in a certain way. Again, if these are working for you, please keep using them. Sometimes, they are just “sugar” for the users to either keep track of problems with earlier prompts or to relieve tension and frustration.

As with any recommendations about ChatGPT prompting, do whatever works for you in your specific situation. I can advise you what I think, but “what works” beats “what someone else thinks works” every time.

A Shallow Dive Into GPT Functionality

Whilst we are maintaining a “black-box mindset” which avoids focus on the internal operation of ChatGPT, it’s important to address some existing concepts of how ChatGPT works. We can’t change the past and unfortunately there are many misconceptions and myths out in the world that we must correct.

Some (Not Unreasonable) Myths on GPT Operation

There are many misconceptions about the internal operation of the GPT AI engine. As we mention in the “Black-box Mindset” we hold that you don’t need to know the internal operation and this is one example of why we think this way.

But treating something as a “black-box” and expecting people to never think about what goes on inside is unreasonable - and futile.

The Black-box Interface:

First let’s briefly review the simple “black-box interface” again, shown in the diagram below. Depending on what we think is happening inside, our expectations of the response, and our judgement of its quality, will vary.

Common (But Wrong) Expectations

The Database Expectation

A very common expectation held by users is the idea of GPT as a database, as illustrated below.

A database contains data, obviously, in a formal structure. When we issue a query, we expect a response that is predictable and repeatable. In the diagram, every time we query for ID=“999.1” we expect a complete, high-integrity response with all the data we’ve requested associated with the id “999.1”. The data may vary from the last time we performed the query, but that’s only because the data in the database changed, presumably under some form of control. The data is authoritative.

The API Expectation

Similarly, if we make an API call, we expect the result to be consistent with the API specifications.

The Web-server Expectation

Similarly on the World Wide Web, a “GET” command with the Http protocol results in the return of a specific resource, assuming the URL is correct.

In all three of these use cases, the data we are querying is stored at the target location (database, API server or Web Server). The query process is to obtain a copy of the data using whatever protocols we use.

This is a simple concept to grasp and many of us are exposed to this type of query every day.

Neither the API or HTTP pattern is how ChatGPT generates responses.

A simplified view of how GPT Generates its Responses

So, we’ve looked at some common expectations that are not how GPT generates responses. Let’s look at a ‘simple’ equivalent that shows how GPT does generate its responses. The situation with ChatGPT (specifically, the GPT AI model that sits underneath ChatGPT) is radically different.

All that is stored in the GPT are word association probabilities - consistent with the black box mindset, let’s leave it at that. If you really want to dig into it, check out Appendix “Advanced GPT Internal Operations” in the book “Advanced ChatGPT Application Primer”.

Let’s imagine a situation where every word in the english language had a probability link we every other word in the language.

It sounds complex, but it’s nowhere near as complex as GPT’s network of word association probabilities.

Now, the “data” that we see in our responses is generated on the fly by GPT based on a complex mash-up of the words in our prompts and data with the probability network in GPT’s network.

So all those little coloured dots are my representation of tokens sitting in the probability network, and GPT decides how to serve them up to you assembled “on the fly” based on your prompt and input data.

It’s really quite amazing how it all works. As I mentioned it’s far more complicated, but the two key points to remember are:

  1. No Persistent Data: GPT is not retrieving blocks of stored facts or text, in the manner that perhaps Wikipedia is doing on a page-by-page basis.

  2. Non-deterministic: GPT applies a mind-numbing number of probability factors - some in large numbers of dimensions - to assemble the response, so it’s unlikely you’ll get the same response twice. Depending on what changes you make to your prompt or context data, you may get highly different responses.

Having a functional model for how ChatGPT generates responses doesn’t break the “black-box” model - it removes misconceptions that would break the black-box model.

Practical Suggestions for Focusing on What the User Can Control

Despite ChatGPT’s black-box nature, users have significant control over their interactions with the model. By focusing on these controllable elements, users can optimise their interactions and derive maximum value from ChatGPT. Here are practical “Do this …” and “Don’t do this …” suggestions based on this component.

Do This:

  1. Define Clear Objectives:

    • Purpose: Clearly define what you want to achieve with ChatGPT to guide your interactions.
    • Example: “Set SMART goals such as ‘Generate a 500-word article on renewable energy trends by the end of the day.’”

  2. Select Relevant Problems:

    • Purpose: Identify specific problems or tasks where ChatGPT can be most effectively applied.
    • Example: “Use ChatGPT to draft emails, create summaries, or generate content ideas.”

  3. Develop an Operational Strategy:

    • Purpose: Plan how to integrate ChatGPT into your workflows systematically.
    • Example: “Create a step-by-step plan that includes implementation, monitoring, and risk management for using ChatGPT in content creation.”

  4. Use Specific and Contextual Prompts:

    • Purpose: Craft prompts that are detailed and relevant to get more accurate responses.
    • Example: “Instead of ‘Explain marketing,’ use ‘Explain the benefits of social media marketing for small businesses.’”

  5. Iterate and Refine Interactions:

    • Purpose: Continuously improve the quality of responses by refining prompts based on initial outputs.
    • Example: “If the response is too vague, add more details or rephrase the prompt to get a better answer.”

  6. Verify Information:

    • Purpose: Ensure the accuracy and reliability of the information provided by ChatGPT.
    • Example: “Cross-check facts and data from ChatGPT with trusted sources, especially for critical or sensitive tasks.”

  7. Manage User Expectations:

    • Purpose: Set realistic expectations regarding ChatGPT’s capabilities and limitations.
    • Example: “Understand that while ChatGPT can generate useful content, it is not infallible and may require manual review and editing.”

  8. Leverage Domain Expertise:

    • Purpose: Supplement ChatGPT’s outputs with insights from domain-specific experts to enhance quality and accuracy.
    • Example: “Use ChatGPT to generate initial ideas or drafts and then have them reviewed by a subject matter expert.”

Don’t Do This:

  1. Assume ChatGPT Is Infinitely Accurate:

    • Risk: Believing that ChatGPT’s responses are always correct can lead to misinformation.
    • Alternative: Always verify critical information independently.

  2. Use Vague Prompts:

    • Risk: Vague prompts can lead to irrelevant or off-target responses.
    • Alternative: Provide clear and detailed prompts to guide ChatGPT effectively.

  3. Expect Deep Understanding:

    • Risk: ChatGPT does not have true semantic understanding or emotional intelligence.
    • Alternative: Use ChatGPT for tasks that require language processing rather than deep contextual understanding.

  4. Over-Rely on ChatGPT for Critical Decisions:

    • Risk: ChatGPT may not provide the depth of analysis required for high-stakes decisions.
    • Alternative: Use ChatGPT as a supplementary tool and consult with human experts for critical decisions.

  5. Ignore the Need for Human Oversight:

    • Risk: Outputs from ChatGPT may require refinement and validation.
    • Alternative: Always review and edit ChatGPT’s responses to ensure they meet your standards.

  6. Expect Consistent Responses:

    • Risk: ChatGPT’s responses can vary even with the same prompt.
    • Alternative: Run multiple iterations and choose the most suitable response.

  7. Treat ChatGPT as Sentient:

    • Risk: Assuming ChatGPT has human-like understanding can lead to misplaced trust.
    • Alternative: Remember that ChatGPT generates responses based on patterns in data, not genuine comprehension.

  8. Overlook Knowledge Scope Limitations:

    • Risk: ChatGPT’s knowledge is limited to its training data and may not be up-to-date.
    • Alternative: Be aware of the model’s training cut-off and verify any recent information from reliable sources.

Conclusion

By focusing on what users can control, such as setting clear objectives, selecting relevant problems, developing operational strategies, and managing expectations, interactions with ChatGPT can be optimised. These practical do’s and don’ts provide a framework for leveraging ChatGPT’s strengths while mitigating potential drawbacks, ensuring that users derive maximum value from their interactions with the AI tool.

Myths, Mistakes, and Biases

There are three main myths or biases that will seriously affect your ability to interact with ChatGPT and make the best use of it. These are:

  1. Anthropomorphising ChatGPT
  2. Thinking that ChatGPT is Hallucinating
  3. Thinking that ChatGPT is Lying to you

These are facets of the same root cause problem: a lack of understanding on the architecture, operation, and capabilities of ChatGPT, or any similar conversational AI tool you may encounter.

Anthropomorphisation

Anthropomorphisation is the attribution of human traits, emotions, intentions, or characteristics to non-human entities, objects, or phenomena. This often refers to perceiving software, robots, and artificial intelligence as having human-like feelings, thoughts, or intentions.

Anthropomorphism can have three impacts in work environments:

  1. Perception of AI: People working with ChatGPT or similar AI models may attribute human-like characteristics to the AI system. This could manifest in ways such as believing the AI system has preferences, feelings, or biases.

  2. Collaboration Dynamics: Anthropomorphising AI tools like ChatGPT can affect the dynamics of teams. If team members view the AI system as another “team member,” they may place undue trust or reliance on its outputs, forgetting the AI system operates based on data and algorithms without true consciousness.

  3. Ethical Considerations: As AI becomes more advanced and integrated into work practices, there’s a need to address the ethical implications of anthropomorphisation. Misunderstanding the AI system’s capabilities or role can lead to inappropriate expectations and potential misuse.

When implementing ChatGPT, it’s essential to educate ourselves and colleagues about the model’s capabilities and limitations. Understanding that the AI system does not have feelings, consciousness, or inherent bias can help in:

  • Setting realistic expectations for the AI system's role.
  • Avoiding over-reliance on the AI system for critical decisions without human oversight.
  • Recognising the importance of human intuition, experience, and collaborative decision-making.

By approaching AI tools like ChatGPT with a clear understanding and setting boundaries, you can harness the AI system’s capabilities without falling into the pitfalls of anthropomorphisation.

Hallucination in AI Systems

Hallucination in AI systems refers to the AI model producing outputs that are not based on its training data or make little sense in a given context. Essentially, the AI system “hallucinates” information that wasn’t explicitly provided or inherent in the input.

Hallucination can have three impacts in AI use situations:

  1. Accuracy of Information: In most work environments, accuracy, and timely information are crucial. If ChatGPT or other AI models hallucinate, it can lead to misguided decisions or misinformed stakeholders.

  2. Project Predictability: In environments that rely on iterative cycles and feedback loops, hallucinated data or insights can disrupt this cycle and lead to unpredictable or undesired outcomes.

  3. Stakeholder Communication: When communicating with stakeholders, presenting AI-generated insights that may have elements of hallucination can erode trust and confidence in the technology.

It’s essential to be aware of the potential for AI hallucination and have mechanisms in place to validate and cross-check AI-generated insights. AI users can:

  • Use multiple sources of data or tools to corroborate AI outputs.
  • Always have human oversight, especially when using AI outputs for decision-making or communication.
  • Educate team members about the nature of AI models, ensuring they’re aware of potential inaccuracies.

By being proactive and knowledgeable, you can leverage the benefits of conversational AI like ChatGPT, while minimising risks associated with hallucinations.

ChatGPT and the Phenomenon of “Lying”

When individuals claim that ChatGPT “lies,” they are often referring to instances where the model produces outputs that are factually incorrect or not entirely aligned with reality. However, it’s essential to understand that ChatGPT doesn’t have intentions, consciousness, or the ability to deliberately deceive.

Users of ChatGPT should factor in the following factors when considering the potential source of incorrect data:

  1. Lack of Conscious Intent: ChatGPT operates based on patterns in its training data. It doesn’t “think” or have intent in the same way humans do. What may seem like a lie is usually an artifact of its training or a misinterpretation of the prompt.

  2. Limitations of Training Data: If there are inaccuracies or biases in the training data, the model might reproduce those inaccuracies in its outputs.

  3. Overfitting and Generalisation: ChatGPT may sometimes overfit to specific patterns or generalise from its vast dataset, leading to outputs that aren’t entirely accurate for a given prompt.

Even when ChatGPT responds to you about its intentions, or apologises for lying, it is just generating text based on its context. It’s not admitting to lying. Keep these three things in mind:

  1. Trust in AI Outputs: If you develop the perception that ChatGPT as deliberately producing “false” information, it can lead to reduced trust in the tool.

  2. Decision Making: If you’re involved in data-driven decision-making, then inaccurate outputs from ChatGPT can potentially misguide you and your stakeholders.

  3. Feedback Loops: If you’re involved in iterative development and continuous feedback, then misinformation can disrupt these feedback loops.

Take the following steps to address any actual or perceived inaccuracies:

  • Cross-check crucial AI-generated insights with other sources.
  • Fine-tune or retrain the model with specific, relevant datasets if possible.
  • Understand the importance of precise and clear prompting to get accurate outputs.

By understanding the mechanics behind ChatGPT’s outputs and implementing robust validation processes, users can optimise their use of conversational AI while ensuring project integrity.

How should you engage with ChatGPT?

If you use ChatGPT in your professional work, you have both a great opportunity and a significant risk. The opportunity is the exceptional amplification of your capabilities, even when using a “simple” tool like ChatGPT. By “simple” I mean the nature of the conversational interface, not the underlying code and data.

The risk is reputational: if you screw up the application of ChatGPT outputs, you can be a contributor to impairment or failure of your work processes or even brand damage to your employer or customer.

Please be smart about how you use it.
