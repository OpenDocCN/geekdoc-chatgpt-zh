Chapter 1 – AI-Assisted Writing - Copyright & IP Ownership

I use AI to edit and improve my writing. I do not use AI to write my books and short stories. Although I have seen many AI instructors advocate this exact use.

For some writers, working with AI tools comes with mixed feelings. The allure of using these platforms to brainstorm, outline, or draft is undeniable, yet they are concerned about creative ownership of their work. Lately the issue of data privacy and concern that AI has the potential to steal creative ideas. Let us look at these two issues further.

Copyright and AI-Assisted Writing

The question of whether AI-generated content can be copyrighted is currently being debated in courtrooms around the world. These court rulings will help set legal precedents, and new legislation may follow based on these rulings, but for now, this is how I see the copyright status of AI-generated and AI-assisted content.

I am not a lawyer; this material on copyrights is based on my own research and opinion and should not be taken as legal advice.

Copyright Basis

Copyright is granted automatically once your art is fixed in tangible form. This is true whether you render a piece of art, write a story, write a song, or record a piece of music. Copyright is an “automatic right.” However, it is much easier to prove you are a legitimate copyright owner if you register your work with the US Government.

Why You Should Register Copyright Your Creative Work

To register your work with the US government, you will need to fill out an application, pay a filing fee, and provide a copy of the work. The copyright office will examine your application to ensure the work is eligible for copyright protection. If so, you will get an official “Certificate of Registration.”

Registered copyright protection makes it easier to pursue legal recourse if anyone uses your work without your consent. If someone tries to use your creative work without permission, you can bring them to court for copyright infringement. 

Some Believe AI Creative Work is Not Copyrightable

The idea that AI-rendered work is not copyrightable may come from the following text in the US Government’s regulations on copyright.

Government Copyrightable Authorship

This text is on chapter 300, page 7 of the US Government’s “Copyrightable Authorship: What Can Be Registered,” see the link below.

https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap300/ch300-copyrightable-authorship.pdf

By following the strict letter of the law as stated by the US Government, the AI-rendered work can be modified by you, which invokes human authorship of the work, and thus, it becomes copyrightable.

Modification of AI Rendered Work

Any meaningful modification of the AI-rendered work can create human authorship. Thereby making the work “AI-assisted” rather than “AI-generated.” AI-assisted creative work is copyrightable.

Registered Copyrights For Me and You

Yes, you can copyright your AI-assisted content.

As a writer looking to copyright your work, it’s crucial to understand these nuances and be vigilant when incorporating AI into your creative work. To keep your creative work copyrightable, my suggestion is to use AI as your writing assistant and collaborator and NOT as a sole content generator.

Stealing Your Ideas

Another concern some writers have in using AI is stealing your writing ideas. Most AI platforms, including ChatGPT and Perplexity, retain user conversations for a period of time—even after a session ends. While users can often delete their conversations, it doesn’t necessarily mean the data is entirely erased from the company’s internal systems. In some cases, conversations may still be used in a limited way to help improve the AI model. For writers, ideas are precious, and understanding where those ideas might travel is essential.

Some of us, myself included, intentionally allow our conversations with AI to remain stored. This lets me revisit discussions to ask follow-up questions or spin off fresh ideas from previous sessions.

How much control do we have over our ideas and data once they’re in the hands of a third-party system?

Ethical AI companies have policies aimed at protecting user data and intellectual property. Still, the legal landscape surrounding AI and creativity remains murky. Despite a company’s best intentions, training data for these models might still contain copyrighted material or generate ideas strikingly similar to those of another user. This isn't necessarily due to malicious intent; rather, it’s an inherent risk of how AI is developed.

It’s also worth noting that, even if a specific conversation remains private, the AI may produce comparable ideas for other users based on patterns from its training data. For writers, this means approaching AI tools with a sense of both opportunity and caution. The benefits are considerable, but so are the potential risks to idea ownership and privacy.

In the end, writers must weigh the creative advantages AI can offer against the possible impacts on their intellectual property. By understanding the issues involved, you can make an informed decision to use (or not use) AI as a creative partner. For myself, I am not concerned with my AI conversation being used as training data.

Prompt Classifications - Open, Fixed and Conversation (Chat)

Open - Open prompts allow the AI to generate the most creative responses. For example,  “Write 300 words on the advantages of Solar Energy.” The topic can vary, such as the best foods for diabetics, diets, etc.

ChatGPT did an excellent job writing 300 words on the advantages of solar energy.

Fixed - This prompt asks for a specific fact or facts. For example, “Who was the third President of the United States?” Keep in mind that the AI can hallucinate facts and data, so remember to double-check its data output.

Chat - Creating a character response with ChatGPT. In this scenario, my 13-year-old character, Tommy, has a problem Explaining to his doctor dad why he failed a biology test. I asked ChatGPT to give me a few ideas of how a character may respond to a dilemma. ChatGPT provided six possible options.

It’s Not Just One (Bite of the Apple) Prompt

I see many people writing prompts and ending the conversation with AI after one prompt. No. You can continue asking and refining your question and drilling down until YOU are satisfied with the answer.

Segmenting AI’s Large Knowledge Base

The AI has a tremendously large knowledge database. We can obtain better responses by telling the AI we want it to segment its knowledge to an area we are interested in. Many prompts of this type begin with the famous “As an expert [ you name the topic].

Establish a Persona For The AI

In addition to the “As an expert” prompt, we can prompt the AI to assume a Persona. This persona could be a doctor, detective, marine, cop, astronaut, dock worker, etc. In writing, you can ask the AI to assume a persona and to respond or ask how the persona would react in your fictional situation.

Establish Your Audience

It can be necessary for the AI to know its audience. Explaining a medical issue to an adolescent is different than to an adult and different from other medical professionals.

Establishing the AIs Tonality

We touched up this before. Do we want the AI to respond in a neutral, formal, informal, friendly, and confident manner?

Background

Giving the AI some set-up and setting will improve its response. Imagine the difference in a marine’s response when fighting in a jungle, urban city, or spaceport 50 light years from the Earth.

By incorporating this information into your prompt, the AI’s response will be focused and on target.

Keys to Creating Great Prompts-      Wash, Rinse, and Repeat

2) ChatGPT will process your prompt and write its response in the chat area.

4) Refine your original prompt to obtain better results.

Chapter 6 - Writing Non-Fiction; ideas, outline, and purpose

Non-Fiction Work

If you are using AI to assist in writing non-fiction material, be careful. AI can sometimes “hallucinate” information, providing inaccurate or fabricated information in such great detail as you would unquestioningly consider it true.

When researching a medical topic, I have had an AI completely fabricate medical facts with fake citations to non-existent studies and papers with fictional non-working URLs. I was stunned by the details involved in the fabrication by the AI.

We, unfortunately, have a tendency to believe computers and AI when they are quoting facts. Always verify any facts generated by AI. With AI, trust but verify.

Plagiarism

AI tools are designed not to copy existing content verbatim, it’s wise to run AI-generated text through a plagiarism checker as a precaution.

Chapter 9 - Writing Emotional Scenes

The AI can help write emotional scenes in two main categories. One is to describe the emotions your character might feel in a given situation. The second is to write the emotional scene itself.

Let’s take the first situation, describing your character's emotions.

Prompt:

Describe the anxiety a nervous person feels on a roller-coaster ride as he is being pulled up vertically to the first drop.

Results:

As a nervous person is pulled up vertically to the first drop of a roller coaster, they may experience a whirlwind of emotions and physical sensations. Here is a description of the anxiety they might feel:

  1. Anticipation and Dread: As the roller coaster climbs higher, the person might feel a growing sense of anticipation mixed with dread. The slow ascent builds tension, and they may become hyper-aware of each click of the chain pulling them upward.

  2. Racing Thoughts: Their mind could be racing with thoughts about what is to come. They might be questioning their decision to get on the ride or imagining the drop ahead with a mix of fear and excitement.

  3. Physical Sensations: Physically, they might experience a rapid heartbeat, shallow breathing, and a tightening in their chest. Their stomach could feel like it's tied in knots, and they may have sweaty palms or a dry mouth as their body responds to the perceived threat.

  4. Heightened Senses: As they reach the peak, their senses might feel heightened. The view from the top, the sounds of the ride, and the feel of the wind can all seem more intense, contributing to their anxiety.

  5. Tension and Release: The tension reaches its peak as they near the top. There's a brief moment of weightlessness and suspension just before the drop, which can feel both terrifying and exhilarating.

  6. Fight or Flight Response: Their body might activate the fight or flight response, a natural reaction to fear, leading to a surge of adrenaline that amplifies their alertness and awareness.

Overall, the experience of being pulled up to the first drop on a roller coaster can be a complex mix of fear, excitement, and physical arousal, culminating in a powerful rush of emotions as they brace for the plunge.

General Guidelines for Selling Print Books on Amazon

Amazon requires a 40% discount off the retail price for softcover books printed using its Print On Demand (POD) service. The POD cost is typically a bit higher than what you can pay to have the books printed by a book printer. You can use Amazon’s POD service inside your KDP account.

On the other hand, if you wish to sell your own printed books on Amazon, you must open an Amazon Advantage account (that’s different from your KDP account). Using your Amazon Advantage account, you can submit your printed book to be sold on Amazon. Amazon must approve the book. Using this avenue, Amazon requires a 55% discount off the retail price of the book, and Amazon does not pay for the shipping of the book to the Amazon warehouse.

Amazon will issue you a purchase order for your book. If the book is only selling a few copies, Amazon will only purchase a few copies at a time. Mailing or shipping one or two books at a time can be prohibitive, depending on your printing costs and profit margin.
