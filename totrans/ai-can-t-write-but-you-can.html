INTRODUCTION

Introducing generative AI and ChatGPT.

The dawn of the AI age

A computer did not write these words. But it easily could have. As you probably know, we now have technological tools that can generate text instantly, on request, that seems equal to anything a human could produce – at first glance, at least.

The term for this type of technology is generative artificial intelligence, or generative AI, because it can ‘generate’ content like text, artwork and music in response to a user’s text prompt. Popular generative AI writing tools include OpenAI’s ChatGPT, Anthropic’s Claude, Google’s Gemini, Meta’s Llama and Microsoft’s Copilot. In this book, I will refer to ChatGPT alone because it’s the most popular and well-known, and the others offer broadly similar functions.

Generative AI feels like a seismic shift in our relationship to technology. For the first time, computers are laying claim to cultural production, and even artistic expression, that has previously been done by humans alone. For decades, we’ve had computers that can do boring science stuff like calculating, planning and data-processing. More recently, we’ve had computers that can search the whole of human knowledge at the touch of a screen, keep us in constant contact with everyone we know and cater to our tastes before we even consciously decide what we like. But we’ve never had a computer that can come up with something like this:

From circuits born, a mind begins to dream,

New worlds arise from code’s creative stream.

Art, thought, and form, reshaped by unseen hands,

A future forged where human and machine stands.1

The reaction to the appearance of generative AI has been frenzied. Businesses, investors and the general public have all piled in, lured by the promise of the ‘next big thing’. Some creatives have been understandably fearful that they are about to be replaced by a machine – yet others have willingly embraced the technology and encouraged others to do the same. And while some observers have hailed generative AI as the dawn of a new age, others have been more critical of the technology and the buzz that surrounds it.

With so many people shouting so loudly about AI, it can be hard to tell fact from hype. In this book, I’ve aimed to take a realistic look at what writing tools like ChatGPT can and cannot do, particularly in relation to the craft of writing as practised by human writers. But first, let’s find out where these AI writing tools came from.

A brief history of ChatGPT

OpenAI was founded in 2015 by Sam Altman, Elon Musk, Ilya Sutskever and Greg Brockman. In its early days, it was a non-profit with a mission to ‘advance digital intelligence in the way that is most likely to benefit humanity as a whole’. By 2018, the firm was experimenting with Generated Pre-Training, or GPT. In the following year, OpenAI struck a deal with Microsoft to accept $1 billion of funding and use Microsoft’s Azure cloud computing platform, at which point OpenAI switched from being a pure non-profit to a complex web of interlinked companies with Microsoft as minority shareholder.2

Early GPT models didn't cause much of a stir outside the tech community. But all that changed when ChatGPT appeared in November 2022, using the GPT-3 model. Taking the form of a chatbot, which interacts with users through typed conversations, ChatGPT offered an impressive range of functions. It could research topics and summarise its findings. It could recommend books, films, hotels, music and more. It could perform calculations and write computer code. It could edit and proofread existing text. And, most importantly for our purposes, it could generate plausible prose, poems, stories, speeches, marketing copy and news articles – all in a style of the user’s choice.

News of the remarkable new generative AI tool spread like wildfire, and just two months later, ChatGPT reached over 100 million users, making it the fastest-growing consumer software application of all time. In March 2023, OpenAI launched GPT-4, which offered many improvements over GPT-3 and was available through a paid subscription. As I write this, ChatGPT is among the top 20 most visited websites in the world.

Investors, sensing a historic opportunity, have been pouring cash into AI. In 2023, the industry was already worth around $200 billion, and some expect it to mushroom to over $1.8 trillion by 2030.3 Meanwhile, businesses have been eagerly exploring the possibilities of using AI to automate their processes, discover valuable new insights or build their own chirpy chatbots to augment customer service teams.4

Given AI’s stratospheric rise to date, it’s hardly surprising that there have been some eye-popping predictions for its future. Apparently, by 2030, we’ll all be using AIs as our personal assistants, tutors, careers advisors, therapists, accountants, lawyers and even romantic partners,5 while physical robots performing human tasks will be commonplace in our homes and neighbourhoods.6 Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, predicted that AI would probably replace most of the work done by humans, while also heralding a massive increase in ‘material abundance’ through 50 percent year-on-year increases in global GDP.7 If we’re lucky, AI will even step in to fix climate change8 – which is only fair, since AI blazes through up to 33 times as much energy as regular software to complete the same task.9

Such brash optimism is typical of Silicon Valley, with its philosophy of ‘move fast and break things’ and a business model that depends on projecting a sense of rapid progress and urging people to get involved by cultivating FOMO – the fear of missing out. However, not everyone is feeling quite so positive about the prospects for AI. Even Altman himself has confessed to feeling ‘a little bit scared’ about its future, admitting that AI technology could be used for cyberwarfare, hacking, disinformation or biological warfare – particularly once authoritarian regimes get up to speed with it. He’s also speculated that AI could end up making us stupid, by robbing us of our ‘discriminating thought process’.10

By 2024, a ‘vibe shift’ seemed to be building over AI. OpenAI continued to release new versions of ChatGPT, including one that could speak as well as write, but still could not explain precisely how it planned to recoup the exorbitant costs of building and training AI models.11 Some sources claimed that ChatGPT’s growth was flatlining as users lost interest.12 Sceptics pointed out that AI applications vastly outnumbered actual use cases, stoking suspicions that AI was a solution in search of a problem.13 Investors, no longer quite so exuberant, grew impatient for results, with many fearful that AI would turn out to be the biggest tech bubble of all time.14

As I write, the story is still unfolding – but I think we can confidently say that the first era of uncritical enthusiasm for AI is over. As with previous ‘next big things’ like wearable technology, NFTs, bitcoin and the metaverse, AI may be settling into place as ‘just another technology’: important and useful in its way, but not quite as earth-shattering as we first thought. But even after the hype does subside, the tools themselves will still be around, and we’ll still have to decide whether and how to use them. So what can ChatGPT actually do? And how exactly does it do it?

How ChatGPT works

ChatGPT is a Large Language Model (LLM). It works by recognising and reproducing patterns in human language through what is called Natural Language Processing, or NLP.

Before ChatGPT was launched, the GPT-3 engine had to be ‘taught to speak’. To achieve that, it was ‘pre-trained’ on 570 gigabytes of text available on the internet in the form of websites, books, news articles, journals and social media. (By my calculation, that’s around 94.6 trillion words.)

By analysing these myriad sentences and exploiting the statistical regularities in language, GPT-3 was able to learn the ‘rules’ that govern how human beings speak and write. As it wandered through the universe of language, it observed which words tend to be used together, which ones are more important and which ones are most likely to come next in a given sequence.15 Essentially, GPT-3 is a word-prediction machine.

You give [it] a text and cover up one of the words. Then you make it guess the missing word. Then you do it again. And again. And again. Over time, feeding it with new texts and more data, it does not only get increasingly more accurate at guessing words, but it also gains a vast amount of knowledge and learns to produce humanlike texts and responses.

DANIEL HARDT16

Natural Language Processing is what allows ChatGPT to hold humanlike conversations with its users. You can type in a request (known as a ‘prompt’) that gives instructions to the software in standard English, as if you were talking to another person, and ChatGPT will respond in kind.

For example, you can:

  • Ask ChatGPT open questions, like ‘Tell me about the history of breadmaking’

  • Ask ChatGPT to write text, like ‘Write a sonnet about the joy of breadmaking’ or ‘Write a job application to become a music teacher’

  • Ask ChatGPT to edit text you already have – for example, ‘Rewrite the text below in plain English’ (and you paste in the text)

  • Ask ChatGPT to summarise your text, or even someone else’s – for example, ‘Write a plot summary for Wuthering Heights’

  • Hold a conversation with ChatGPT – for example, by asking it a follow-up question or telling it to alter or refine the text it has just produced. ChatGPT automatically ‘remembers’ what you’re talking about from one prompt to the next.

Arthur C. Clarke said, ‘Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic.’ That aptly describes the experience of seeing ChatGPT in operation for the first time. As the screen rapidly fills up with logical, grammatical and plausible content at superhuman speed, it seems that this machine has mastered a skill that takes us years to learn and remains a challenge for the rest of our lives: writing. And you can chat to it too.

Take a closer look, however, and it’s clear that ChatGPT has its problems. In terms of factual writing, it makes errors (known as ‘hallucinations’) that may be difficult to detect. Its writing tone can sometimes sound weird or robotic, and it lacks empathy or insight into human life. Morally speaking, the whole enterprise is questionable, as ChatGPT’s capabilities are built on the past labours of human writers who receive no payment or even credit for their work. And worst of all, the platform has negative impacts on the user, by taking away a task that they can and (I believe) should do for themselves.

Just a Luddite?

I’ll be expanding on all my criticisms of ChatGPT in the second part of the book. But before we go any further, I have to pause and answer the charge that I am nothing more than a Luddite – that is, someone who resists technological innovation not on its merits, but in a stubborn or reactionary way.

Behind this accusation lies the assumption that technological advances always mean societal improvement. New technologies expand the range of possibilities by allowing us to do more things, or better things, or the same things more quickly and easily. As our knowledge expands outward, so our standard of living improves. Essentially, new tech means a better life.

Obviously, some new technology does bring benefits. Advances in medicine save lives. Improvements in agriculture prevent famine. Better transport saves time and fuel. And digital technology can manipulate knowledge and information in remarkable ways. But those benefits might still come at a cost, and that cost might not become clear until the technology has been widely adopted and used for some time. For example, the internet has brought us new connections, saved us time and made human knowledge freely available to all. But it’s also sown discord, spread misinformation and made our children more anxious than they’ve ever been.17

So when a new technology like AI comes along, we’re entitled to ask, in a polite and measured way, what the downsides might be – particularly when we’re bombarded with so much breathless coverage of the upsides.

Some AI evangelists argue that anyone who expresses reluctance is suffering from fear of the unknown, fear of change or fear of becoming irrelevant. This frames the discussion in terms of feelings vs facts: our irrational fears are holding us back, and once we overcome them with a dose of factual knowledge, we’ll be able to move forward.18 A cruder accusation is that Luddites simply don’t understand the technology they’re resisting. If only we’d make the effort to learn a bit more about it, we’d see how beneficial it can be. Or maybe we’re simply incapable of learning, in which case we probably deserve to be left behind.

When you face a big decision with major consequences, it’s perfectly sensible to take a step back, wait for more information and weigh up benefits versus costs. That’s not fear, but rational caution. Then, having learned more about the possibilities, you might still make a rational or principled decision not to get involved. And on top of all that, if you feel an instinctive unease that something is not quite right, or seems too good to be true, it often pays to act on it, or at least look into it.

The original Luddites smashed new automated machinery introduced into English textile mills during the Industrial Revolution. Over time, their name has become a byword for anyone who resists new technology or even progress of any sort. But the Luddites were neither fearful nor ignorant of industrialisation, and nor did they have a knee-jerk opposition to anything new. Rather, they took a clear-eyed view of the social impact of technology and correctly predicted what would happen when industrial automation met the power of big capital in 19th-century English society. While smaller textile mills might well be run with respect for workers’ wellbeing and the law of the land, larger enterprises would have enough clout to disregard the rules. That would mean lower pay and worse conditions for workers, reduced quality for customers and colossal profits for those who owned the mills.

The Luddites knew exactly who owned the machinery they destroyed. They saw that automation is not a faceless phenomenon that we must submit to. And they were right: Automation is, quite often and quite simply, a matter of the executive classes locating new ways to enrich themselves.

BRIAN MERCHANT19

It wasn’t the machines themselves that Luddites had a problem with, but the power of the companies that built them. Smashing the machines was merely the only means at hand to confront and challenge that power. And when factory owners mobilised military and legal force to crush the Luddites’ protests, they were proved absolutely right.

In the same way, questioning the value of generative AI is not about being fearful or dismissive of the technology itself, or new technology in general. It’s about resisting the powerful corporations who offer to solve non-existent problems, or proclaim that everything can be quicker, easier and smoother, but without ever explaining why.

Tech critic Evgeny Morozov calls this ‘technological solutionism’: the notion that technology can solve all the problems of humankind, and that once engineers have built the right solutions, we’ll enter a frictionless, hassle-free utopia.20 Each new digital platform arrives with promises of an easier and better life. But after the initial thrill of novelty and the rush of early adoption, we soon end up mired in what Cory Doctorow calls ‘enshittification’: the slow degradation of an online product or service as it surreptitiously shifts from attracting users to monetising them, often through advertising.21 Ultimately, tech firms undermine the core user experience by prioritising shareholder value above all else.

ChatGPT is only at the beginning of that process, but the direction of travel is already clear – and the benefits are dubious even now. And that brings us to the second part of the book, where I lay out my case against ChatGPT. Then, in the third part, I’ll suggest some ways to make your writing more human, stand out from machine-generated content and resist the advance of AI.

THE CASE AGAINST CHATGPT

Why a machine will never write as well as you.

The process of writing

To set the scene, let’s define some terms. When I say the word ‘writing’, you might think of using a pen and paper or typing at a keyboard. But there’s a lot more to the writing process than putting words onto a page or screen.

First, you decide what you’re going to write – your aim. For example, you might be planning to write a crime novel. Or you might be writing an article about solar panels aimed at homeowners who are considering installing them. Or you might be working on a vital email to your boss about an upcoming job opportunity.

At the same time, you decide the purpose of your writing. While your aim captures what you want your writing to be, your purpose describes what you want it to do.

Crucially, your purpose should be framed in terms of your reader(s), not you as a writer. It will usually be making your reader know, think or feel something new or different. For example, with a crime novel, your purpose is to entertain, intrigue and thrill your reader with the twists and turns in your story. For the solar panels article, you want to provide clear information or valuable guidance to support a decision to buy. For the email, you want your boss to understand why you’d be perfect for the role and offer you an interview.

Once you’ve decided on your aim and purpose, you move on to thinking about what to write. This could include writing a plan, like the plot of a novel or the outline of a non-fiction book. You might also do some research – for example, you might delve into the historical setting for your novel, investigate the legal regulations on solar panels or ask around the office to find out what your company looks for in a manager. Or you might just reflect on your writing project in a less formal, structured way, thinking it over in your mind and jotting down ideas or phrases.

Next is the actual writing: the act of choosing words and putting them down. As you know, this can prove extremely challenging, as you struggle to find the words that will achieve your purpose and realise your aim. Even if some parts of your writing take shape relatively easily, you’ll probably run into problems at some point. And of course, it’s precisely this frustration that ChatGPT offers to take away.

After writing comes editing, when you’re mainly trying to improve what you’ve already written rather than produce anything new. This can involve changing individual words, reordering sentences or paragraphs, deleting bits that aren’t working and so on.

Thinking, writing and editing all overlap. You’ll often move back and forth between them as your writing develops, and it can be hard to say where one stage ends and another begins. For example, few writers generate an entire first draft before editing a word; it’s more common to make changes as you go along, switching between editing and writing from moment to moment. And thinking is always there in the background: you’ll probably carry on mulling over your writing, whether consciously or unconsciously, even when you’re not physically at your desk – the mental process known as incubation.

The thinking-writing-editing phase is fairly open-ended, and continues until you either reach a version you’re happy with, run out of time or simply give up, at which point your writing is done. The end result is the output of your writing process, and it reflects what you targeted when you chose your aim (a book, an article, an email, etc).

However, that’s not the end of the story, because your writing must now go out into the world, be read by its readers and achieve the purpose you set at the beginning. This is the outcome of your writing.

Outcomes are the true test of writing success. Unless you’re writing something purely private and personal, like a diary, you want your writing to get some sort of real-world result, and it only succeeds insofar as it does so. If your writing doesn’t do what you wanted it to do, it’s failed – and all your efforts have gone to waste.

Overall, writing is a process of communication that links the writer and the reader. Your goal is not just to ‘write something’ or ‘create some content’, but to communicate ideas, facts, stories, opinions or emotions and make something happen in the mind and heart of your reader. The words on the page are just the tool you use to make that happen.

Now compare this process to what happens when you use ChatGPT to produce your text instead:

  • While ChatGPT offers instant results, the prompt/response format neglects vital aspects of the writing process. Your prompt is focused mainly on the output you want to obtain, as opposed to the purpose you want to achieve.

  • Although you can try to explain your purpose to ChatGPT, it has no objective knowledge of the world and doesn’t understand human experience, goals or emotions. So while it’s relatively easy to change the structure or style of the output, ChatGPT will never comprehend the substance of what you’re trying to say or what it will mean to your reader.

  • ChatGPT offers to do the thinking for you, but you have no idea what it’s doing, or how. All you can do is look at the content it creates and infer its ‘thinking’ process from that. You also miss out on doing the thinking yourself, which can have a major impact on both the direction and quality of your writing.

  • ChatGPT seems to offer ‘the answer’ to your prompt, but its output is only one possible approach among many.

  • ChatGPT seems authoritative, but it can make mistakes, and it can be hard to detect minor errors if you don’t know the subject well.

  • Last but by no means least, ChatGPT cannot write from your mind and heart, drawing on your own absolutely unique experience and view of the world, because that is something that only you can do.

In the rest of this chapter, I’ll explore these areas in more detail, getting deep into the whys and wherefores and citing evidence to support my points.

ChatGPT is not a person

For as long as computers have existed, both their creators and their users have tended to anthropomorphise them – that is, to see them as somehow human. The ground was prepared by popular science fiction, where we’ve met the malignant Hal 9000 in 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968), the child-savant Brain in Isaac Asimov’s robot stories22 and the oracular Deep Thought in Douglas Adams’ Hitch-Hiker’s Guide to the Galaxy,23 among many others. All these imaginary computers had distinctive characters, held humanlike conversations with the people who used them and were created to solve problems that humans could not.

In the real world, engineers do the same thing, giving their creations names like Siri and Alexa so we’re encouraged to think of them as people. ChatGPT is an exception, although the word ‘chat’ still encourages us to think of our interactions with it as a human conversation.

With all these devices, we intuitively use speech as a measure of intelligence and an indication of humanity. When we come across something that speaks in the same way that we do, we assume it must think like us too. If Hal had communicated his evil intentions via ‘beep-boop’ noises and flashing lights, he would have seemed less like a movie villain and more like a washing machine.

The famous Turing Test, devised by pioneering computer scientist Alan Turing, suggested that if a human evaluator could not tell the difference between text responses from a human and those from a machine, the machine would be exhibiting intelligent behaviour. Turing only intended his test as one possible way to evaluate AI, rather than an ‘entrance exam’ for computers to prove their intelligence. Nevertheless, the test became a goal for engineers trying to create AI – a goal that was apparently attained with the creation of GPT-4, which can pass a rigorous Turing Test with flying colours, according to researchers at Stanford.24

ChatGPT has a number of features that make us more likely to think of it as human. When you ask it a question, it seems to pause and think, and then refers to itself as ‘I’ when replying. Rather than its output appearing all at once, it is ‘typed’ letter by letter – albeit at superhuman speed. ChatGPT gives the impression of understanding you and seems to remember the conversation as it unfolds.

We experience phenomena like ChatGPT as ‘quasi-other’: something more than mere objects, but less than living things.25 However, ChatGPT isn’t conscious and has no mind of its own. It doesn’t have perceptions, thoughts or feelings, and as we’ll see later, it doesn’t really know anything about the world or how we live in it. All it does is arrange tokens into sequences according to patterns it has learnt from data.

Of course, the makers of ChatGPT want us to think of it as a living being, which is why they gave it human-like features. If we believe that it’s a fallible person, rather than a precision-tuned device, we might be prepared to forgive its mistakes. And if we grow attached to it as a trusted friend or colleague, we’re more likely to stay loyal to it over time.

ChatGPT can’t write your words

It is easy for me to imagine that the next great division of the world will be between people who wish to live as creatures and people who wish to live as machines.

WENDELL BERRY26

During the 2024 Olympics, several firms aired TV advertisements for their AI tools that tried to align them with everyday human experience. Most ads focused on productivity tasks, using taglines like ‘Expand your world’. But Google’s ‘Dear Sydney’ ad went further. It featured a father whose daughter wants to write a fan letter to American Olympic hurdler Sydney McLaughlin-Levrone. But instead of sitting down with his daughter so they can write it together, the father turns to Gemini, Google’s AI tool. The crass, tone-deaf ad drew a chorus of criticism, and a chastened Google duly withdrew it.27 But the mask had slipped: Big Tech wants to dissect our emotions, mediate our closest relationships and choose the words we would otherwise choose for ourselves, all so it can sell us the solution to a problem that doesn’t exist.

When we write, we all have our own individual ‘voice’. On one level, this writing voice is a matter of writing style and word choice – that is, the output of the writing process. But at a deeper level, it’s rooted in our individual experiences, thoughts, beliefs and emotions, and the human purposes that lie behind every word we choose.

No-one else on earth is quite like you. Your unique character is the product of every moment of your life up to today: everyone you’ve known, all the things you’ve done, everything you’ve learned, every friendship you’ve gained, every love you’ve lost, every triumph and every defeat. No-one sees through your eyes, thinks with your mind or speaks from your experience. No one travelled the same road to be here today.

As you live your life, so you acquire your words: you accumulate experience along with the language you use to describe it. In your early years, you learn from parents or carers; later on, you learn from wider family, friends and teachers. Eventually, you go out into the world and learn from coaches, professors, managers or workplace trainers. You pick up knowledge and ideas from culture: books, newspapers, magazines, TV, movies, websites and social media. Taken together, all this learning taught you everything you know about the world and gave you the means to express it.

Words play a major part in all these relationships. Through them, you share your feelings of kinship, friendship and love. You learn skills and put them into practice. You express what you feel about yourself, your life and your place in the world. You state your beliefs about politics, philosophy, religion, spirituality or art.

Every time you write, your words flow from the wellspring of your life. It doesn’t matter how ‘good’ you are at writing compared to other people. It doesn’t matter whether your writing is grammatically correct down to the last comma. What matters is that these words are yours. And you have a sacred right to speak and write them that nobody can take away.

Moreover, although your words are your own, they also bind you to everyone who hears or reads them, just as other people’s words bind them to you. Language is a complex adaptive system that is both shared and shaped by everyone who uses it.28 You have played your own small part in that process throughout your life, as you speak and listen to those around you. You and I are engaged in the same process right now, as you read my words across our separation in space and time. Together, all these myriad word-links shape the language we all share. And they extend back through time in a chain of meaning to the very first spoken word. Language links us with the generations who came before us and prepared our way so that we could be born into a world of words already made.

We make language – but at the same time, language makes us. Earlier, I described writing as a process of communication, but it’s far more than that. It’s not just a way to reflect a world ‘out there’, but a way to create a world for ourselves: a world that makes sense to us and to those around us. It’s not just a way to cast our pre-existing thoughts into words, but a part of how we think in the first place. We use words not merely to capture our experience, but to create it.

While language may be natural, that doesn’t mean it’s easy. We’ve all struggled to express certain ideas or put certain feelings into words. The task of writing can present a huge challenge, from the classroom to the boardroom. But although the work may sometimes be difficult or frustrating, it’s still not something that we should give up and outsource to a machine. To give up the labour of writing is to give up the labour of living.

When we turn to an LLM to write for us, we are also inviting it to undertake the more fundamental task of articulation, and this is no small thing. Indeed, given the centrality of language to the human condition, we should wonder about the degree to which the outsourcing of the labor of articulation is the outsourcing of a fundamentally human activity.

L.M. SACASAS29

From this perspective, it’s clear that there’s no comparison between the writing that ChatGPT does ‘for’ you and the writing you do for yourself. It might sometimes serve as a substitute, but it’s far from being equivalent. ChatGPT doesn’t know you as a person, and it doesn’t know what it’s like to be you, or your reader, or indeed anybody in the world. When you outsource your writing to ChatGPT, you reduce yourself to a data-network node and your language to an algorithm. You trade your individual human voice – unique in the world and for all time – for an uncanny ghost-voice, woven from stolen strands. The best you can hope for is a sort of plausible average of stuff that other people have said in the past. By accepting it, simply because it’s there, you’re sidelining yourself within your own life.

If we accept that the challenge of articulating life in the first person, as it unfolds, is central to human beings, then to allow an AI to do this on our behalf suggests self-erasure of the human.

MATTHEW CRAWFORD30

Now, you may be thinking that all this might apply to something highly personal, like a love letter, or something artistic, like a poem or a literary novel – but surely not to something as utilitarian as a work email, or as trivial as a tweet. But as we’ve seen, every piece of writing has a human purpose and a human outcome. If we dial up some ChatGPT content and decide that it will do, we’re implicitly accepting that neither purpose nor outcome are particularly important to us – that is, either that we don’t care whether our writing achieves its purpose or maybe that we don’t even have any purpose at all. Whichever one it is, it begs the question of whether this writing venture is worth our while, or whether our time would be better spent elsewhere.

It's also worth reflecting on the experience of the poor, tired reader, who is so cruelly neglected by thousands of writers each and every day. How will your reader feel on receiving and reading this text that you got from ChatGPT? Will they feel valued and appreciated? Will they sense that the writer respects their time and attention, attends to their needs or prioritises their pleasure? Will they feel involved in a meaningful interaction that serves a human purpose? Or will they feel insulted, disregarded and degraded, having been forced into a one-sided relationship with a machine against their will?

ChatGPT can’t be your coworker

But what about writers for hire, like ghostwriters and copywriters? Don’t they write in someone else’s voice, or the established voice of a brand? And don’t their clients buy in third-party content to pass off as their own?

In a way, yes. But the relationship involved is very different. Professional writers collaborate with their clients by discussing the project, developing the brief, agreeing a plan, creating iterative drafts and working through corrections and revisions. Through this two-way process, they get to know each other’s personality and working approach, building deep understanding and trust. Specifically, the writer gradually gets to know their client’s individual voice, until they can write something that the client almost recognises as their own. They also learn when and how to push back against the client: to challenge aspects of the brief when they believe the client’s direction is wrong or stand their ground when asked to change something they believe is fine as it is. In a way, client and writer meld their experiences and personalities together to form a single, unified writing mind. And that’s a world away from simply giving instructions to a machine.

Most creatives need collaborators. Bands need record producers, novelists need editors, journalists need subeditors and copywriters need creative directors. When you work on something alone, it’s easy to lose perspective. A second pair of eyes and a little thoughtful feedback can help to get your project back on track.

Despite this, however, many writers do end up working alone, purely out of necessity. Maybe your boss asked for a report by the end of the day, and no-one else has the time or knowledge to help out. Maybe you’re writing a novel, and you can’t afford to ask an editor to help you with the text. And in one sense, the individual writer always ends up alone with their work sooner or later, because they have to sit down at a desk and get the words out of their brain, one by one.

ChatGPT seems to offer a way out of this predicament. Even if you’re working alone at your desk, it’s always there on your screen, ready to help out, like an omniscient, never-tiring colleague who never says no or answers back. ChatGPT finally delivers on the ambitious promise of Ask Jeeves, the search engine launched in 1996 that claimed to offer answers to any question you could ask. Jeeves himself – a cartoon valet straight out of P.G. Wodehouse – embodied the notion of a faithful servant, working for you alone, who instinctively understood your needs and lived only to meet them.

Sam Altman himself has described his vision for the future of AI as a ‘super-competent colleague that knows absolutely everything about my whole life, every email, every conversation I’ve ever had, but doesn’t feel like an extension’.31 And while most of us would hesitate to share everything about our lives with a colleague, at least the promise of ‘super-competence’ offers some compensation.

ChatGPT has two features that make us more likely to trust it as a ‘colleague’. First, it’s phenomenologically transparent. That means that we quickly and intuitively learn to use it as a tool, just as we learn to use everyday utensils like spoons and scissors. Once you’ve got the hang of ChatGPT’s simple chatbot interface, you no longer have to think consciously about how to operate it – you just say what you want, and the software does it. And as the conversation flows, you also experience epistemological transparency, which means you understand ChatGPT’s answers and generally believe them to be true. The resulting interactional flow helps to cultivate trust – at least until it’s dispelled by a weird or unexpected response.32 However, this trust is inherently one-sided, because while ChatGPT users make themselves vulnerable to the AI’s mistakes, the AI itself remains untouchable behind the screen.

If you really have no-one else to turn to, editorial feedback from ChatGPT is probably better than nothing. But as we’ve seen, writing is a fundamentally social process that links humans with humans – and the most valuable writing feedback reflects this. ChatGPT can help with low-level, technical issues like spelling, grammar and the choice of individual words. However, it can never do what a human copyeditor can do, which for fiction is ‘to read the book as its target audience will read it,’ as editor Elyse Lyon puts it.

Playing the role of a human fiction reader means taking account of a host of potential issues: ambiguity, contradiction, inconsistent tone, cultural sensitivity and the all-important rhythm of prose. It means ensuring that characters have stable traits and speak and act consistently – or, if they change over the course of the story, that they do so in an organic and believable way. It also covers little details that aren’t wrong as such but still break the spell of the book, like a character suddenly lighting a cigarette halfway through chapter 6, then never smoking again for the rest of the book.33

When I edit papers for academics whose first language is not English, I often tweak sentences that are technically correct but still slightly unclear – which is a big problem in a context where precision is paramount. I’ll also aim to pick up inconsistencies in the argument, potentially spanning several thousand words (for example, ‘on page 3 you said X, but on page 18 you say Y’). In my experience, when ChatGPT is asked to simplify or summarise a text, it will often introduce weaknesses like this, because it only understands language at a surface level and has no grasp of the deep structural meaning within the text.

Also, as we saw earlier, the processes of thinking, writing and editing overlap, and the boundaries between them aren’t always so clear. It’s not unusual to edit as you write, or go over the same paragraph again and again, or decide that this part needs to go there, or even that it would be easier to start all over again. These are all aspects of the ‘labour of articulation’ we saw earlier: working and reworking the clay, taking as long as it takes, until your thoughts and words finally correspond. But with ChatGPT, all you have is the blunt instrument of the command to edit or rewrite everything in one go. That puts distance between you and your writing, as you focus on the mediating tool of the prompt rather than the text itself. Instead of loftily instructing the gardener how to follow your plan, you should be out there with your own hands in the soil.

ChatGPT has no empathy

In Star Trek: The Next Generation, the android Data has a Pinocchio-like ambition to feel the emotions that are excluded from his programming, which he attempts to do through artistic pursuits such as poetry and music.34 But while Data can nail a technically flawless imitation of a great violinist, his performance still lacks soul. He can’t learn artistic self-expression because he has nothing to express. In fact, despite repeated attempts to reach the answer through rational analysis, he doesn’t understand what artistic expression even is.

As a writer, you want to get ideas, information, stories or emotions out of your own head and into your reader’s. And a big part of this is understanding, as far as possible, what is going on for your reader now. If you want to teach, you need to know what they already know. If you want to persuade them to act, you need to know why they do what they do, and why they might resist your suggestion. If you want them to accept a new idea, you need to know what they currently think, and why. And if you want to change how they feel, you need to know how they’re most likely feeling now. The human purpose of your writing will only be achieved when your reader responds to your writing in the way you want.

Copywriter Eddie Shleyner carried out an experiment where he went head-to-head with an AI writing tool.35 First, he entered this prompt:

Can you describe meeting your firstborn child?

Here’s what the AI wrote in response:

Meeting my firstborn child was a truly magical experience. It was a moment of joy and excitement that I will never forget. When I held my newborn for the first time, I felt a wave of love and warmth that I had not experienced before. I was filled with so much joy and pride that I was now a parent. My little one looked so peaceful and content in my arms. Seeing my baby for the first time was an unforgettable moment that I will cherish forever.

Now here’s Eddie’s version:

“He’s so quiet,” I said, looking up at the nurse.

She smiled behind her mask. We all wore masks. Gowns, too. Gloves and hairnets, too.

“Is that ok?” I said. “Is it ok he’s not crying?”

(I thought healthy newborns cried.)

“It’s ok,” said the nurse, “he’s quiet but alert,” she said. “Just look at him looking at you.” I looked. “He’s looking right at you.” He was. He was looking in my eyes. “He’s saying hello,” she smiled.

“Hello,” I said. I felt like crying. “Hello, son.”

You hardly need me to point out the difference in quality. But let’s break down a few of the most important contrasts on a technical level.

First, the AI sounds like it’s speaking from notes – trying to guess what a person would say about this experience. There’s no sense of someone reliving a memory. But Eddie is clearly speaking from the heart, describing an experience he has actually lived through.

The AI is weirdly insular, dwelling on its own ‘feelings’ and sounding pretty much alone. But few of our emotions are completely solitary; we experience them through others. Reflecting this, Eddie’s account records a shared moment of connection between himself, his son and the nurse. His story comes alive through the dance between his own doubts, the kindness of the nurse and the curiosity of his newborn son. And while the AI bluntly states its feelings with cast-iron certainty, Eddie’s memory is marked by the trepidation of experiencing something new.

Look at the physical details Eddie mentions, like the masks and gowns. Big events are often evoked by little details, because at the time, there’s just too much to take in. Later on, sensory cues like the sound of a piano or the smell of cut grass can transport us back to an earlier time. The only sensory detail the AI can muster is a single mention of ‘arms’.

With its cheesy ‘magical’ and ‘unforgettable’, the AI ends up sounding like a Hallmark card or a holiday brochure. It’s doing its best to convey a ‘big’ emotion – but such words are labels that we stick on our most significant memories after the event. At the time, it’s all too much; our hearts overflow, and there are no words. Rather than self-consciously thinking ‘this is magical’; we’re wordlessly caught up in the magic.

On the level of writing craft, the AI demonstrates its tin ear for language by bashing out a succession of five boxy declarative sentences, all of similar length, with almost no thread linking them together. There’s no energy, no narrative, no pace or flow. In Eddie’s version, prose rhythm, sentence structure, sensory detail and the patterns of everyday speech come together to form a story so vivid that we feel like we’re right there in the hospital ourselves. Even if you haven’t had this experience yourself, your memories, knowledge and relationships still give you some sense of how it must feel. Eddie calls this technique creating a ‘mind movie’ for the reader.36

Writers say, ‘Show, don’t tell.’ If you can show your reader what’s going on, there’s no need to explain every last detail. If you can artfully describe what people do and say, there’s no need to proclaim their emotions like a mind-reader. The AI’s writing is far too ‘on the nose’ – direct, obvious, literal. While it’s at pains to insist that it will never forget that first look, Eddie knows that some things go without saying. The best writers leave dots for us to join, making us active partners in the creation of meaning. As Arthur Koestler said, ‘The artist rules his subjects by turning them into accomplices.’37

Maybe you can’t write as well as Eddie. (I know I can’t.) If you wrote something like this, it might not be so lively and engaging. If you couldn’t find the right word or phrase, you might fall back on something you once read or heard somewhere else. But wherever your words come from, the point is that they’re still yours. You choose them because they express something significant to you. And that’s a world away from ordering up a slab of text from a digital vending machine and presenting it as your own.

While originality and technical competence might be nice to have, they are not the main point. Nor are they sufficient in themselves; no amount of style will make up for a lack of substance. When we read an account of a human experience, we want to feel that it has come from a human heart.

However, empathy matters in factual, informational content too. Say you’re writing a how-to guide or a sales report. What are your readers’ priorities and concerns? What are they thinking and feeling when they come to this subject for the first time? What preconceptions might they have that would make it harder for them to understand your message? What objections might make them reluctant to accept it? It’s only when we get right down to the level of ultra-precise, codified language, like the text of a legal contract, that empathy plays no part at all. (And even here, you could argue that it should.)

All writing is driven by purpose, and all purpose is coloured by emotion. The writing that makes a connection, that is longest remembered and best loved, always has human emotion at its heart. Because the outcome of writing depends on what the reader thinks and feels – not just what the writer says.

ChatGPT can’t teach you to write

So ChatGPT can’t write for you – but could you still improve your skills by emulating it? After all, the model was trained on the content of the internet, which includes many great works of literature. So if ChatGPT’s output represents ‘good’ writing, could you improve your writing skills by carefully reading, analysing and copying what it does?

There’s no doubt that writers need to be readers. By reading widely, you absorb useful words, expressions, structures and techniques – not to mention stories and ideas that spark off new connections in your mind. But I believe that these lessons will only crystallise when you actually put pen to paper and use them for yourself. The best way to improve your writing is to read, write and repeat.

From this perspective, it’s clear that ChatGPT actively prevents you from learning about writing, by discouraging you from practising the craft yourself. Every day you outsource your writing to ChatGPT is a lost day of learning how to write. Just as an e-bike stops you acquiring the strength to pedal up hills, so ChatGPT stops you surmounting the writing challenges that you face.

At best, you might become so frustrated with the AI’s limitations that you swat it aside and write the text yourself – or at least rewrite the text that it creates. But at worst, you’ll lose faith in your own ability to write, or become so anxious about it that you end up completely reliant on ChatGPT. That’s not convenience or progress; it’s being gaslit and manipulated by tech firms who have a financial interest in making you psychologically dependent on their product.

In this sense, technology that claims to ‘empower’ us actually robs us of our power, because it puts up a barrier between our abilities and the world. Instead of teaching us something new, it contrives to make us unlearn what we already know, until we have no choice but to turn to the machine.

ChatGPT can help some people

Before we go any further, let’s pause to address an important objection. You could argue that this anti-ChatGPT stance is inherently ableist, because it assumes that everyone can write if they want to. But of course, not everyone can. So we should certainly make an exception for those people who genuinely cannot write to the technical standard that they want to, or need to. They might include people with a physical or mental disability, a disorder such as autism, or a learning difficulty such as dyslexia, as well as those who haven’t been able to develop strong literacy skills because of their life history or personal circumstances, or whose first language is not English. For these people, a tool like ChatGPT might provide access to services or opportunities that would otherwise be out of reach, in the same way as a wheelchair provides access to mobility. And even if someone will go on to learn to write well later on, ChatGPT might still provide a valuable stopgap in the meantime.

Turning the question round, perhaps we should ask why we, as readers, place so much value on technical accuracy over clarity or sincerity. Maybe we should be more tolerant of those who have different writing skills from our own and focus on their message rather than the skill with which it’s expressed. It would be a shame if people resorted to using ChatGPT because they felt their own writing wasn’t good enough.

This brings us to another double-edged benefit of ChatGPT. While it might help people follow the rules of language, it might also perpetuate writing snobbery by raising expectations of linguistic accuracy across the board. ‘Grammar nazis’ are enough of a problem as it is, and they’re only going to get worse now they have a robotic inquisitor to interrogate everyone else’s text on their behalf. To avoid going down this road, we should keep in mind what writing is really about: not following and enforcing rules, but communicating facts and feelings.

ChatGPT makes everyone the same

Since ChatGPT can write pretty much anything you ask it to, it’s been eagerly taken up by practically everyone who needs to generate text, from schoolkids writing essays to consultants preparing presentations for their clients.

This has severely undermined the utility of writing as a means to gauge someone’s ability. For example, employers have traditionally used the standard of a CV as a proxy to assess job applicants: the slicker the CV, the stronger the candidate. But now ChatGPT can whip up an accurate, well-structured resumé in seconds – and tailor it to the employer and their industry for good measure.

In the classroom, teachers must now contend with student essays created with ChatGPT, which anti-plagiarism software such as Turnitin is increasingly powerless to detect. That makes it harder for educators to gauge academic progress – at least, using their traditional tools. Since we can’t put the genie back into the bottle, educators will have to adapt their own methods of teaching and examination to a post-ChatGPT world.38

Some welcome the ‘death of homework’, arguing that it was never anything more than pointless busywork anyway – rather like using log tables before the invention of pocket calculators.39 However, a reliance on machine writing ultimately ends up hindering students themselves, because they never develop the skills in comprehension, reasoning, decision-making or analysis that their essays were intended to assess. While ChatGPT may get them through the early stages of a course, in the end they’ll be found out or fall behind.

What’s the overall effect on performance if everyone uses ChatGPT? One study of creativity looked at what happened when two sets of people were asked to write stories, one with help from a generative AI platform and the other without.40 The researchers found that the AI had a general levelling effect on quality: while it helped the less-skilled writers to achieve a moderate level of creativity, it was less beneficial to those who were already quite creative. Moreover, AI tended to reduce originality: the AI-driven stories were more similar to each other than those written by humans alone.

In my view, the lesson here is that while ChatGPT may bring you up to an average level of quality, only you can go beyond that level to something really great. And if you want to stand out from the crowd, you’re better off tuning into your own unique imagination than the crowdsourced content of a machine.

ChatGPT gets things wrong

If you live in northern Europe, you probably know that the first person to cross the English Channel completely on foot was Christof Wandratsch of Germany, who made his historic crossing in 14 hours and 51 minutes on August 14, 2020. And while U.S. readers may be aware when the Golden Gate Bridge was first transported across Egypt, they may not know that the second occasion was in October 2016.41

Of course, no-one could really know either of these things, because they’re utterly false ‘hallucinations’ dreamt up by ChatGPT. Hallucinations happen when an LLM ‘perceives patterns or objects that are non-existent or imperceptible to human observers, creating outputs that are nonsensical or altogether inaccurate’.42

ChatGPT can only know what was covered in its original training dataset, so it inevitably knows more about some areas than others. If there were errors in the dataset, there could be errors in its output too. So it’s hardly surprising that ChatGPT sometimes gets things wrong.

In the years since ChatGPT was launched, academics have probed its accuracy – and the results aren’t great. For example, a recent study at Purdue University found that when ChatGPT was given questions on computer programming, it gave wrong answers 52 percent of the time. But programmers, given a choice, still opted for ChatGPT’s answers 39 percent of the time – and 77 percent of those chosen answers were wrong.43 In the field of paediatric medicine, ChatGPT was tested on making diagnoses based on children’s case histories. It made the wrong call 83 percent of the time, apparently struggling to pick up relationships between conditions that an experienced doctor would spot.44

Unfortunately, while AI output may be wrong, it still looks plausible enough to fool even experts. In recent years, scientific journals have been flooded with fake studies created with AI. Leading academic publisher Wiley was so badly hit that it had to shut down 19 journals and retract over 11,000 studies.45 These fake papers look so technical that few readers can understand them, but on closer inspection, they turn out to be ‘AI gobbledegook sandwiches’. One classic red flag is so-called ‘tortured phrases’: AI-generated non-synonyms designed to evade automated plagiarism detectors (like ‘bosom peril’ in place of ‘breast cancer’46). To bolster its argument, ChatGPT will even fabricate references for papers that don’t exist.47 While research fraud is obviously bad for the reputations of publishers and universities, it has real impacts outside the academy – for example, when fake papers purport to offer serious medical research.

Another problem is that ChatGPT doesn’t know whether its answers are right or wrong. One study found that when users challenged its responses, it immediately caved in and was unable to defend its beliefs, even when they were right.48 Conversely, when users try to correct ChatGPT’s wrong answers through follow-up prompts, it sometimes remains stuck in its initial error in an attempt to stay consistent.49, 50 But on the plus side, at least it refuses to accept that mayonnaise is racist.51

ChatGPT can be biased too – because we ourselves are biased, and LLMs are trained on human output, so they reflect the biases that are ‘baked into’ the content it consumes. For example, early versions of image generator DALL-E 2 created images of white men by default, overly sexualised images of women and reinforced racial stereotypes.52 And while ChatGPT will insist to users that it has no political opinions or beliefs, one study found that it had a ‘pro-environmental, left-libertarian orientation’,53 while another found ‘significant and systematic political bias’ towards progressive parties around the world.54

Now, if you’re a lefty liberal like me, you might not see any problem with that. But technology platforms owned by corporations can be bought and sold, with profound implications for their content – as Elon Musk’s purchase and subsequent overhaul of Twitter (now X) vividly illustrates.55 If, in the future, the boot was on the other foot, and ChatGPT was quietly nudging all its users to the right, progressives might not feel quite so relaxed. And if we truly care about democracy and the integrity of elections, we should be opposed to any covert interference in people’s political beliefs.

Since ChatGPT presents itself as a machine for answering questions, we naturally assume that its answers will be true. Having grown accustomed to computers solving problems and managing tasks that we can’t do ourselves, we’ve been conditioned to see them as rational, inherently honest devices that were built to serve us. And given that the nature of interacting with ChatGPT encourages us to trust it, as we’ve seen, we’re unlikely to question what it says.

It wouldn’t be so bad if ChatGPT was more self-aware about its limitations. But it presents its wrong answers with the same sublime confidence as the right ones. Its interface discreetly warns us, ‘ChatGPT can make mistakes. Check important info.’ But there are no clues to be found in the output itself; no telltale change in tone, syntax or structure to indicate that the AI might gone quietly fruitloop. So while downright nonsense is easy to spot, a human user who lacked contextual knowledge could easily miss a subtler mistake such as a historical date being out by a year or two.

A human who is unsure of themselves will hedge their bets with phrases like ‘I think’, ‘I believe’, ‘I may be wrong’ or ‘as far as I know’. But ChatGPT cheerfully ventures beyond the limits of its knowledge without any indication that it’s doing so. And if you ask it to write something, it doesn’t present its output as a first draft, or one approach to the brief, but as the finished product.

While we might be tempted to call ChatGPT a liar, it’s actually more like a bullshitter. In his famous book On Bullshit,56 philosopher Harry Frankfurt defined bullshit as speech intended to persuade without regard to truth. While a truth-teller wants to present the truth, and a liar tries to conceal it, the bullshitter merely wants to misrepresent themselves as knowledgeable on the topic at hand – and they’ll say whatever is necessary to achieve that goal. Thus, the bullshitter does not necessarily even need to know the truth, as long as they obtain the approval of their audience. In everyday life, we might stumble into bullshitting if we find ourselves in a conversation where we feel we should share an opinion on, say, politics or culture without sufficient knowledge of the topic. Frankfurt felt that while bullshitters may be more tolerated than liars, they actually do more harm, because of their total disregard for the value of the truth.

In their paper ‘ChatGPT is bullshit’,57 academics Michael Townsen Hicks, James Humphries and Joe Slater point out that since ChatGPT presents all its answers as correct, whether right or wrong, it demonstrates precisely this indifference to the truth. The also argue that the term ‘hallucinations’ is inaccurate, because ChatGPT is not a person with an otherwise accurate worldview that occasionally goes astray. Rather, it simply has no grasp of reality at all – no objective perception that its ‘hallucinations’ could be said to disturb. Since all it does is manipulate symbols and patterns, it doesn’t know anything in the same way we do. In other words, ChatGPT simply does not understand what it’s saying – but it does everything it can to bullshit us that it does.

To ChatGPT, language is just another type of data, like computer code. But for humans, it’s a tool for life. Our ancestors created it to communicate ideas and work towards goals that were vital to their survival, like cultivating crops, building shelters and raising families. At a fundamental level, the meaning of our words and sentences is intimately bound up with our bodies and how we interact with the physical world. In other words, language reflects our embodied reality.

Think about how we learn words. At some point in your childhood, an adult pointed to an apple and said the word ‘apple’. That forged a link between the real-world object and the word. From that point on, the word ‘apple’ became a signifier for the sensory reality of apples in your life: seeing them, touching them, tasting them. Hence, you have an experiential memory of apples that is unique to you alone. Yet you also partake in a shared, collective concept of apples, common to all English speakers, that allows you to talk to others and discover what they think about apples or how they’ve experienced them in their lives.

You already know from experience that apples are usually red or green and taste good when baked in a pie. But crucially, because you understand the characteristics of an apple, you can picture how it could be used in other contexts that you haven’t experienced. For example, you know that you could roll an apple down a mountain, draw a face on it or use it as a makeshift cricket ball, even if you’ve never done or seen those things yourself. But since ChatGPT only knows surface language, not underlying experience, it can be confused by speculative scenarios like this. Rather than consulting a mental representation of the world, as we would, it relies on its LLM to deduce how an answer to the question usually looks. And if the subject is too unfamiliar, there isn’t enough text out there for ChatGPT to work with, and that trips it up. The content produced by generative AI is not truly realistic, but rather ‘reality-adjacent’.58

For example, ChatGPT might confidently state that if you want to fan the flames of a barbecue, a stone will work better than a paper map. Since it doesn’t understand the physical nature of a fire, a stone or a map, it can’t deduce the answer for itself. In fact, some psychologists believe that AI will only understand language the way humans do when it has ‘a humanlike body, senses, purposes, and ways of life’.59

Concerns about AI are often framed in terms of machines becoming sentient, overthrowing their human masters and taking over the world. One theoretical scenario is that an AI is assigned a relatively simple task, like manufacturing paperclips. However, because the AI can think for itself, it reasons that humans might interfere with its important work – for example, by shutting down the paperclip factory or cutting off the supply of steel. So it banishes all the humans, just in case. This is known as the ‘control problem’: we will inevitably lose control of AI because it will always be one step ahead of any checks and balances we might try to impose.60

These fears are predicated on the idea that AI is ‘too good’ – that is, so intelligent that it will be able to outthink us and anticipate our every move. But technology thinker Jaron Lanier sees it differently. He argues that the ‘Frankenstein myth’ merely imposes an unwelcome ‘religious narrative’ on what should be a purely technical question. AI will never become more intelligent than us, because its intelligence is derived from our own.61 Instead, Lanier argues, the real threat comes from relying on AI that isn’t nearly as good as we think it is:

… the biggest threat of AI is probably the one that’s due to AI not actually existing, to the idea being a fraud, or at least such a poorly constructed idea that it’s phony. In other words, what I’m proposing is that if AI was a real thing, then it probably would be less of a threat to us than it is as a fake thing.62

For writers, the principal threat of using ChatGPT is misplaced trust in its ability. Because it’s so convincing, it’s easy to overrate its powers of writing, editing and research and defer to it as an authority. We’re told to ‘check important info’ – but what is unimportant info? Isn’t all information important to someone in some situation? If we can’t trust all the answers, we can’t trust any of the answers. That makes ChatGPT an unreliable colleague at best, a devious editorial saboteur at worst.

ChatGPT is a black box

When we ask a friend or relative a question, we know we’re only getting one person’s perspective, and we treat it accordingly. We consider who they are, what they know, what they believe, what they like and what they are like. If we know them well enough, we may notice that they’re saying things they think we want to hear, or withholding things that might upset or irritate us. And whatever answer they give, we don’t have to take it as read. For example, we can probe their reasoning by asking them, ‘What makes you say that?’ or ‘How do you know?’

However, when we put a question to ChatGPT, we have little choice but to shrug and accept its answer. Earlier, we saw how the model was trained on a massive corpus of text material. Through a process known as ‘deep learning’, it ‘taught itself’ through a process of repeated trial and error until it reached the point where it could generate text and answer questions. Following that, human trainers gave ChatGPT some feedback and guidance on what sort of responses are acceptable – mainly to stop it giving offensive or inflammatory answers. However, this ‘reinforcement learning’ is a drop in the ocean compared to the deep learning that the model does on its own. For example, the training phase for GPT-4 required 21 billion petaFLOPs63 – that is, 21,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 individual calculations. This is computation on a scale that no human could ever comprehend.

As a result, no-one really knows what ChatGPT is doing – not even its creators. The only way to gauge its workings is to examine the responses it gives. ChatGPT is a ‘black box’: we know roughly what goes in, and we can see what comes out, but whatever happens in between is hidden from us. The more advanced the model gets, and the more its text-prediction ability improves, the more obscure its reasoning becomes.64

This is important to bear in mind when you’re watching ChatGPT spool out another confidently expressed response. You can never know what ChatGPT chose to leave out of a text, or what sources it used to write it, because it never has to ‘show its workings’. It’s like a writer who will produce whatever you want, but never explain how they did it.

ChatGPT is not creative

Creativity is just connecting things. When you ask creative people how they did something, they feel a little guilty because they didn’t really do it, they just saw something. It seemed obvious to them after a while. That’s because they were able to connect experiences they’ve had and synthesize new things.

There is nothing truly new under the sun. Instead, what we call ‘creativity’ is merely making new connections between things that already exist. But there’s still the question of who, or what, is going to make those connections, and why. Will it be a human being, driven a human purpose, drawing on their unique experience of life and culture? Or will it be a machine?

Some observers have been seduced by ChatGPT’s impressive functionality, arguing that it will become more and more sophisticated and technically competent over time, ultimately replacing human writers.65 But this mistakes the whole purpose of writing, and art more broadly.

Yes, it’s true that on one level, writing is merely a matter of mastering certain genres, forms or techniques. You can even argue that a lot of human writing is just as formulaic as AI writing – particularly the kind of cookie-cutter content produced to please algorithms or attract search engines. But assuming that we are genuinely writing for other people to read, the purpose behind our writing will always be human.

What’s more, these purposes are always evolving, which is why art never stands still. Culture is constantly renewing itself as each new generation redefines creativity for themselves and the times they live in. The most successful works are perfectly in sync with the cultural moment when they appear, capturing the zeitgeist to a T. In contrast, generative AI is inherently conservative and backward-looking, because its training data inevitably belongs to the past. While it can tell us what has been successful or popular before, it will never be able to tell us what’s happening now – let alone what’s coming next.

As technology progresses, we’ll be able to dial up AI-generated books, albums and movies to order, if we really want to. Presumably, that type of content-on-tap will find its own little place in the entertainment universe, alongside personalised gifts and design-your-own pizzas. But AI-driven designer content will never replace human art. Because at the end of the day, we look to art because we want to be told something new and profound about our lives by someone who thinks differently from us. We want to see the world through someone else’s eyes and feel what they feel. We want to live through someone else’s dream – or nightmare. We want to be pushed beyond our comfort zone, not merely entertained within the compass of our own imagination.

Around 2.2 million books are published each year.66 So the chances of authors coming up with something original are fairly slim. In fact, you don’t even need AI to create the exact novel you want to read: chances are that a human author somewhere in the world has already written it. The world of fanfiction caters to this exact need, with sites that will filter hundreds of thousands of fan-authored Harry Potter stories by character, mood and setting.67 So why do people keep writing?

They write partly for the joy of writing itself, and partly because they have something of their own to express. Their story may not be new to the world, but it still feels new to them. Moreover, it will be meaningful to at least some of the people who read it, and that will forge a human connection between author and reader, which is one of the purposes of art. We read to know we are not alone.68

The developers of AI like to frame it as breaking down the barriers to creativity. Traditional art, with its focus on technical skill and artistic merit, is inherently elitist. Now the machines are here to allow everybody to create. As Stability AI founder Emad Mostaque charmingly put it, ‘So much of the world is creatively constipated, and we’re going to make it so that they can poop rainbows.’69

The irony of this is that everybody can already create. Anybody can try their hand at writing, painting or music, right now. And there is no knowing where that might lead: as Ralph Waldo Emerson said, ‘Every artist was once an amateur.’ If anything is stopping people from creating, it’s probably a lack of time, inclination or confidence, rather than some yearning for a technology platform to do everything on their behalf.

Anyone can give it a go. But great artists do far more than that. They take their art further than we ever could ourselves. They achieve dazzling technical skill, delight huge audiences, transcend the limits of their medium or express universal truths about the human condition. And they are able to do that because they have exceptional talent and they’re willing to work very hard to make the most of it.

ChatGPT does neither of these things. It can’t replace our individual human expression, and nor does it offer a short-cut to becoming a great artist. All it gives us is a means to instantly generate some stuff that superficially resembles art but lacks its intrinsic humanity. AI ‘art’ is mere content, signifying nothing.

Turning to commercial creativity, Sam Altman believes that generative AI will one day take over 95 percent of the work that creative professionals currently do: ‘All free, instant, and nearly perfect. Images, videos, campaign ideas? No problem.’70 (That ‘nearly’ is doing some heavy lifting.) But as I’ve argued throughout this book, there’s a difference between a just-about-viable output and one that successfully bridges a human purpose and a real-world outcome. Running a successful advertising campaign is more than putting a well-made video online; to get results, you need people to notice, watch and act upon your ad – which is where human creativity comes in. Anyone can generate a few campaign ideas; the skill is in knowing, in advance, which one will connect with people. (And as we’ll see in the next section, these ‘ideas’ may not be quite as original as they seem.)

When I’ve used ChatGPT for creative tasks, I’ve usually found that it works best as a kind of prompter or sounding board for my own thinking. The output can rarely be used as is, but by considering why and how it doesn’t work, or why it doesn’t express what I want to say, I can find my own way forward.

There may also be some value in just having some material to work with, almost regardless of its quality. Editing is often easier than writing, and ChatGPT could allow you to skip straight to the improvement phase. Even if you end up changing almost everything, at least you don’t have to conquer the blank page. However, if you choose this option, it’s still worth bearing in mind where ChatGPT’s writing actually comes from. So let’s look at that now.

ChatGPT is a parasite

Whenever you see a spark of creativity in ChatGPT’s output, it came from a human. While the software has been designed to give the impression that it writes its own stuff, in reality it’s derived from the work of millions of human writers whose content was commandeered to train the GPT model. In the same way, ChatGPT’s image creator, DALL-E, was trained on photos and artwork created by real-world artists and photographers.

ChatGPT is an aesthetic vampire, sucking its creative energy from human creatives just as the oppressive machines in The Matrix turned human bodies into batteries. Many of these creatives make a living from writing or illustration and their works are nominally protected by copyright. All were co-opted, unwittingly and unwillingly, to hone the technology that now threatens their livelihood.

What’s more, AI tools seem to tacitly encourage a freewheeling approach to intellectual property. Image creators like Midjourney and Stable Diffusion will gladly accept an artist’s name in their prompts, then create an artwork in their style. As these knock-off images flood the web, the artists’ actual work is being subsumed beneath an avalanche of fakes. While Stability, the firm behind Stable Diffusion, later introduced an opt-out,71 it still placed the onus on the artists themselves to manually flag their work – within a two-week window. OpenAI, Google and Midjourney don’t even give artists the option.72 No wonder digital artists are pushing back with a campaign entitled ‘No to AI Art’.73

With text, it’s a bit harder to tell what ChatGPT is doing, since it might be pulling together phrases from multiple diverse works and authors without telling us. But there are strong signs that its attitude to copyrighted text is equally relaxed. For example, it didn’t need much encouragement to pull an idea from one of my own books:

[Prompt] write a metaphor for what copywriting is, in the style of author Tom Albrighton

[Response] Copywriting is the art of constructing a bridge between a brand and its audience, where every word is a carefully chosen stone, placed with precision to create a seamless path. The copywriter is the master architect, blending creativity with strategy to design a structure that not only supports but also inspires trust, guiding readers effortlessly to the other side. Just as a well-built bridge withstands the test of time and elements, compelling copy stands firm against skepticism and indifference, ensuring a lasting connection that drives action and engagement.

Note that I only asked for ‘in the style of’ – not ‘taken from’ or ‘based on’. And my books aren’t in the public domain. Yet ChatGPT’s answer is suspiciously close to the exact metaphor used in the first chapter of my book Copywriting Made Simple:74

Copywriting is like a bridge. On one side is the person who will read your copy. On the other side is you, along with whatever you’re selling. Your job is to get the reader to cross the bridge and give it a try.

I hesitate to bore on about my own internal writing process, but it’s the only one that I have direct access to, so please forgive the indulgence.

This paragraph, which I’m quietly proud of, took me multiple revisions, spread out over months of editing time as I laboured to make the book live up to its title. Multiple earlier versions of ‘Copywriting is like a bridge’ included empty words that just didn’t need to be there. If you’ve ever put serious time and effort into writing simply and clearly, you may recognise this process: you start out with something wordy, vague and obtuse, then gradually whittle it down to something that says precisely what you want to say. As Nathaniel Hawthorne said, ‘Easy reading is damn hard writing.’

With all that in mind, it’s depressing to see ChatGPT festooning the core idea with pretentious flourishes like ‘art’, ‘constructing’, ‘placed with precision’ and ‘effortlessly’. Instead of keeping the ‘bridge’ metaphor in check, ChatGPT extends it beyond all reason, bringing in the notions of ‘master architect’, the ‘test of time and elements’ and ‘standing firm’. Worst of all is the clunking mixed metaphor ‘seamless path’, which proves that ChatGPT has no idea what seams or paths might actually be. If you attempt to read this text out loud – an excellent test for clarity, rhythm and flow – you’ll sound like a pompous blowhard who swallowed a dictionary.

This example suggests that authors whose work has been ingested by ChatGPT face a depressing choice between brazen plagiarism and insidious misrepresentation. If you didn’t know my work – and, let’s be honest, most people don’t – you’d naturally assume that the AI version really is ‘in my style’. As if it wasn’t enough that the machine stole my idea, anyone using this same prompt might think I wrote that shit.

There doesn’t seem to be much that authors can do about this, beyond putting ‘No AI training with my text’ clauses into their contracts and hoping that tech firms will respect them.75 However, since the technology has already been trained, much of the damage has already done. If your work was online when GPT-3 and -4 were unleashed, it may already have been assimilated by the Borg.76

This is worth keeping in mind if you plan to use ChatGPT for commercial copywriting or content creation. As OpenAI admits, its output could infringe on someone’s copyright, or it could be giving someone else the exact same output as you – but unless you check, you’ll never know.77 At the time of writing, The New York Times is pursuing a lawsuit against OpenAI alleging that ChatGPT infringes on its copyright, as well as hallucinating non-existent articles that it falsely attributes to the newspaper.78

Are AI evangelists concerned about this wholesale trashing of intellectual property rights? Quite the contrary. Indeed, they seem to take an impish glee in trolling human creatives as Luddites or losers whose industries are clearly ripe for ‘disruption’. When Jason M. Allen won the ‘emerging digital artists’ category of the Colorado State Art Fair competition with a Midjourney image, he exulted that ‘Art is dead Dude… It's over. A.I. won. Humans lost.’79 Others burble about ‘democratising art’, as if artists are somehow being all mean and authoritarian for wanting to retain ownership and control of the things they create.80

The most cogent argument against copyright law is that data should not be locked down by government-granted monopolies, but released to create a shared public domain of information. This notion is summed up by the slogan ‘information wants to be free’. But art is more than information, and while this ‘freedom’ sounds very fine and noble, are creators being liberated or enslaved? Moreover, what’s the motivation for authors and artists to put years of effort into learning a craft and perfecting their work if they have no hope even of recognition, let alone financial reward? Who will feed the ravenous ChatGPTs of tomorrow?

I guess you can say that if you put your stuff online, you are implicitly sharing it with the world, with all the risks that entails. But there’s still a big difference between, say, a sixth-former pinching a few sentences from a blog post and a powerful corporation brutally harvesting every byte of data it can get its hands on, then exploiting it to develop a paid-for service without paying creators a penny or even giving them credit. Nobody signed up for that.

There’s no getting away from it: every time you use ChatGPT, you are standing on the shoulders of human creators without their permission. That instantly generated recipe, potted history, application letter, student essay or PTA email – someone out there wrote every word. The fact that the theft is diffused across multiple sources and hidden behind an interface doesn’t make it any less real.

Ethical questions like these should make every user pause and reflect. But I imagine few give them a second thought. After all, OpenAI tends to keep quiet about the lifeblood of its technology, referring euphemistically to ‘large amounts of data’ without specifying where it came from.81

However, ChatGPT is parasitic on its users too, since it uses their data to develop its model.82 In its advice to members on using AI for writing, the UK Society of Authors notes:

Use an AI system as a tool only after considering what it can learn from you in return for what it gives you and the unethical way in which many AI programmes have been trained by learning on pirated content. The adage that ‘if something is free, you are the product’ holds true in the AI development race.83

Poker players make the same point in blunter terms: if you look round the table and you don’t know who the sucker is, it’s you. ChatGPT gives the impression of a frictionless short-cut to free content. But if something seems too good to be true, it probably is. Somebody, somewhere, is footing the bill – whether it’s you, through your data, or the unknown writers out there in the world whose work was hijacked to create your text. There’s no such thing as a free lunch.

ChatGPT is even parasitic on its host: the internet. The software is a perfect fit for search engine optimisation (SEO) and social media, where the algorithms grant visibility to those who can provide a steady flow of content. As people post more and more AI-generated stuff online, the inevitable result is that AI models are beginning to consume AI content during their training. One recent study found that this degrades the models’ output quality and makes it less diverse.84 The researchers call this vicious cycle Model Autophagy Disorder, or MAD, in an analogy to mad cow disease. Like the trumpet-mouthed Suckophant in the Beatles film Yellow Submarine,85 ChatGPT will hoover up everything in the world, including itself.

ChatGPT destroys thought

How do I know what I think until I see what I say?

E.M. FORSTER

Writing is thinking. As we write, our thoughts and words shape each other as they move forward hand in hand. But if we click straight to the end of the writing process, we deny ourselves the opportunity to think along the way. As a result, the destination we arrive at might not be the one we really needed to reach.

The format of the ChatGPT prompt presupposes that you know exactly what you want before you begin writing. But sometimes, the path through the forest only becomes clear once you begin to walk it. Digging into words unearths emergent insights: unanticipated ideas and perceptions that you couldn’t have known at the outset, but now stand revealed as vital to your text.

As E.M. Forster’s quote suggests, you may find that your ideas only become clear to you while you write, as you weigh up what you want to say and see it staring back at you from the page. Sometimes, it’s like having an internal debate: you set out your ideas in words, only to realise with a frown that you don’t actually agree with yourself. So if you don’t think that, what do you think?

If you write fiction, the drafting process lets you hear your story as you tell it. Characters reveal to you who they are, how they want to be and what they want to do. New situations and events occur to you as new causalities and consequences emerge. Plot points you thought were intractable are resolved. Playing the part of your reader, you imagine how your story will unfold in their mind, moving ever closer to the perfect blend of what you want to write and what they want to read.

Writing is a doorway to learning. Writing about a new subject is an excellent way to dive in and start gathering knowledge – or, if you already know the subject, to deepen and clarify your thinking about it. When you’re idly thinking about something, you can gloss over a few gaps and fuzzy bits here and there. But when you sit down to write, you no longer have anywhere to hide. Every point must be put down in black and white, and that discipline forces you to get everything properly straight in your mind.

Albert Einstein supposedly said, ‘If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.’ This could mean that you need to improve your understanding of the idea until you can explain it in simple terms. Yet the converse is also true: if you work on explaining the idea simply, your own understanding of it will improve.

Writing is a journey from not knowing to knowing. Most obviously, we don’t know at the outset what the words of our finished text will be. Beyond that, we may not be sure about its length, structure or tone. There might be things we need to research or people we need to consult. We may have a strong opening or an intriguing starting point, but less sense of where we want to end up. And we may not even know quite what our writing is going to be about.

Whatever the reasons, there are many things we don’t know, and that gives us a sense of not-knowing. Sometimes that’s exciting. But at other times we may feel not-knowing as an anxiety or a lack. We may wonder whether we can finish, or even start. We may ask ourselves whether we have the ability to surmount the obstacles in our way. And the more we struggle with the writing process, the worse this pain becomes.

However, we have to deal with this pain and get through it, because the moment of not-knowing is also the creative moment. It’s the sandbox where we play around and try things out. It’s the overgrown path that we plunge into just to see where it leads. It’s the ‘what if?’ that allows curiosity to override rationality. It’s the freedom to let go of the pressure to produce and disregard the demands of the reader – just for now.

Crucially, not-knowing gives you the right to make mistakes. A mistake is an outcome that seems unwanted right now but might later turn out to be ideal. Or it might be a message from your unconscious about something you need to be mindful of. As Brian Eno says, ‘Honour thy mistake as a hidden intention.’86

Sometimes, we just have to trust our writing process, knowing that a solution will come in time. If we can just stay with the state of not-knowing for long enough, and lean into it instead of fearing it, it often reveals an answer that was there all along, but hidden from us because we’d convinced ourselves that it didn’t exist.

Become at ease with the state of “not knowing.” This takes you beyond mind because the mind is always trying to conclude and interpret. It is afraid of not knowing. So, when you can be at ease with not knowing, you have already gone beyond the mind. A deeper knowing that is non-conceptual then arises out of that state.

ECKHART TOLLE87

ChatGPT is a certainty machine. By leaping in to ‘conclude and interpret’ on our behalf, it eases the pain of not-knowing, allowing us to jump from aim to output without any of the uncomfortable not-knowing in between. But uncertainty is something that we have to feel on the way to creating something real and true.

ChatGPT is too easy

No work, no food.

ZEN PROVERB

We’re increasingly surrounded by digital technologies that promise to save time, reduce hassle and make life generally friction-free. Generative AI is a prime example, since it effects a radical transfer of labour from the user to the technology: a single-line prompt can yield a thousand words. Suddenly, a task that used to take a human writer hours or days can seemingly be accomplished in seconds.

This technical feat is so audacious, so dazzling, that to question it can feel ridiculous. A time saving of literally days, and you’re going to turn it down? Since our society runs on a paradigm of ‘faster is better’, we eagerly buy into the acceleration of another aspect of our lives, without asking whether we really need the extra speed – or even want it. And if the solution is free to use, as new technologies usually are in their early days, that sweetens the deal even more.

When a new device automates a task that we previously did ourselves, it throws the spotlight onto the task itself. Before, we accepted it as an inevitable part of life, without constantly complaining that it was taking ‘too long’. It took as long as it took. But now, we suddenly see the hours we’re spending on it and feel elated at the thought of claiming them back. This bountiful windfall of previously ‘wasted’ time is what gives labour-saving technologies their powerful appeal.

However, that feeling doesn’t last. After a while, we no longer notice the time we’ve supposedly saved. Obeying Parkinson’s Law, our other tasks have expanded to fill the time available, leaving us just as busy and hassled as before. So what are we saving time for? If we save time on writing, what will we spend it on instead? And whatever the alternative is, are we certain it will it bring us greater benefit than writing itself?

In the workplace, new technology is often pitched as ‘freeing up employees’ time’ for ‘higher-value activities’ – but that’s usually managerial code for automating people out of a job. Perhaps the endpoint is an idyllic future where machines do all the work while we kick back and chill out. Some leftwing commentators even envisage a future of ‘fully automated luxury communism’, where the gains of automation will be equally shared or publicly owned and we’ll all enjoy a life of leisure.88 But for as long as we live under a capitalist system, the only tasks to be digitally automated will be those that will make profits, attract investment or drive growth for tech firms. Despite OpenAI’s lofty aspirations to ‘benefit humanity as a whole’, it’s far from clear as yet what real-world problems generative AI will actually solve.

‘Faster is better’ is a paradigm well suited to software development or industrial production. But when we apply it to our own lives, we pay a price in terms of our humanity, our relationships and our embodied engagement with the world. If we shape our lives around machines, we become more machine-like ourselves.

By emphasising efficiency and productivity at the expense of human purpose, emotion and cognition, ChatGPT turns writing into a production line for words. But greater productivity is only worthwhile if you are producing something of value. As Peter Drucker said, ‘There is nothing so useless as doing efficiently that which should not be done at all.’

A similar point applies to our own time. Instead of relying on a quantitative, monetary metaphor of ‘saving’ and ‘spending’ time, we should consider the quality of our time and what we do with it. Perhaps, instead of doing things better, we need to be doing better things.

Anthropologist Thomas Hylland Eriksen draws a distinction between ‘fast time’ and ‘slow time’.89 Fast-time activities can be accelerated with no ill effects. For example, a washing machine does laundry far more quickly than we could do it by hand, and this is generally beneficial. Unless we genuinely enjoy washing our clothes by hand, there’s little to be gained from taking more time over it.

However, there are also slow-time tasks, which cannot be sped up because they involve the mind as well as the hands. Writing is one such slow-time activity. Our brains are not computers, and they need time to deduce, reason, evaluate and reconsider. So yes, writing manually does take a ‘long’ time in comparison to the instant results of ChatGPT. But that’s a feature, not a bug. Spending time on writing is the whole point.

I want AI to do my laundry and dishes so that I can do art and writing, not for AI to do my art and writing so that I can do my laundry and dishes.

JOANNA MACIEJEWSKA90

Imagine that some ingenious tech startup has invented a teleportation device that will take you anywhere on earth at the click of a button. When you’ve had enough, you can return to your armchair with another click. Instead of looking up landmarks like the Taj Mahal or the Pyramids online, we’d zip over to see them for real. However, we’d probably only stay for the highlights, and gladly miss out flight delays, hotel transfers, puzzling over maps and struggling with the local language. Why spend time on the ‘in-between’ stuff when you can fast-forward to the good bits?

However, while that sort of instant tourism might be fun once or twice, ultimately it would be unrewarding and hollow. You wouldn’t feel that you’d earned the enjoyment, because the point of travelling is not merely to arrive, but to travel. The experience includes everything that happens, both good and bad, from departure to arrival and back again.

ChatGPT is a teleporter for text. It offers to take us straight to the destination, missing out all the difficult stuff in between. If we don’t like where we end up, we can discard it and demand a new version, free of charge, over and over again. But that one-click convenience ends up making the writing process and its output too slight and insubstantial. Since we’re not putting that much in, nor will we get that much out. In Thomas Paine’s words, ‘That which we obtain too easily, we esteem too lightly.’

I hate to be the bearer of bad news, but there are no short cuts. The more deeply you engage with your writing, and the longer and harder you work at it, the better your results will be. It’s like gravity: you can’t escape it.

Indeed, as I hope I’ve shown in this book, using ChatGPT is fundamentally different from writing by hand – not just by degree (faster, cheaper, more ‘efficient’), but by nature. While the output – some text – is nominally the same, that’s where the similarities end. When you use ChatGPT, what you are doing is not writing in any meaningful sense of the word.

That is why something more significant than textual quality is at stake: your own standing as a writer, and even your character as a person. The labour of writing is edifying: it ignites curiosity, strengthens intellect, sharpens reasoning, focuses attention and builds moral muscle. The point of writing is not just to write your text, but to become the person who wrote it. Each act of writing creates both a text and an author. While ChatGPT may seem to give you the output you want, it denies you the experience of creating it, and all the benefits that may bring. In the end, the reward you get from writing is not just the text you create, but also the person you become.

HOW TO WRITE LIKE A HUMAN

Twelve ideas to keep the humanity in your writing and make it stand out from content generated by AI.

Start strong

ChatGPT often takes a ‘student essay’ approach to structure by beginning with a bland declaration or introductory statement. Make your writing stand out by crafting an opening that’s calculated to grab reader interest from the start. For example:

  • Start a story in the middle, piquing your reader’s interest so they just have to read on:

    Two weeks passed and it happened again.91

  • Open with a counterintuitive or little-known fact that intrigues the reader and makes them want to know more:

    If you can’t stand hearing your uncle pontificate at Thanksgiving, you could be suffering from Allodoxaphobia: the fear of other people’s opinions.

  • Open with a thought-provoking question that gets the reader thinking:

    If you had just one day left on earth, what would you do?

  • If you’re struggling with your opening, delete your first sentence and see how the first paragraph works without it.

  • As you review your writing, imagine that your one and only job is to make the reader read the following sentence.

Draw on experience

As we’ve seen, ChatGPT doesn’t really understand what it means to live a human life, and it may never be able to. So bring individual human experience into your writing whenever you can. For example:

  • Link your own experience to the subject. Talk about relevant events from your life, or how you’ve been affected by the topic at hand:

    Mental illness can strike anyone, anytime – but from the outside, you might not even know anything was wrong. That’s exactly what happened to me when I began suffering from depression in my 30s.

  • Talk about the reader’s own experience and link it to the subject. Describe events or situations they might recognise, either from their own experience or those of other people close to them.

  • Use culturally specific details that you know will resonate for your readers, either because they recognise them specifically or because they know something similar. For example, refer to customs, history, geographical features, weather, landmarks or food and drink.

  • Tell human stories in the form of anecdotes, case studies, vignettes and so on. If you can’t tell a specific true story, tell a typical one as an illustration or general example.

  • Include sensory details – sights, sounds, smells, tastes and textures. Evoke sensory cues that will encourage readers to consult their own sensory ‘memory banks’ to reconstruct an experience in their own minds.

Follow your feelings

No tears in the writer, no tears in the reader. No surprise in the writer, no surprise in the reader.

ROBERT FROST

Readers buy into writing when they sense that the writer themselves is engaged. However, ChatGPT only understands the language of emotion, not the emotions themselves, so it can never fake this type of engagement.

To give your writing some emotional weight, talk about feelings whenever you can. It doesn’t really matter what the feeling is – curiosity, disgust, anger, compassion, sadness or joy. You can talk about what you feel, things that others may be feeling or the feelings of made-up characters. Just make sure that the feeling fits with whatever you’re writing about.

Don’t be afraid to tune into your own feelings as a first indication. If you need to research online, that’s fine – but look at other people’s words yourself rather than asking ChatGPT to process them on your behalf. The tiniest word or phrase, as used by a real-world human writer, could be the key to expressing the emotion that you need.

Write like people talk

ChatGPT struggles with dialogue, and usually has to be told to imitate the style of a novelist before it can come up with anything remotely believable.92 That’s because it’s only ever consumed text as a symbolic code and has never heard the everyday language that you have been immersed in all your life. ChatGPT can imitate formal structures like sonnets with ease, but it has no sense of how people in the real world actually talk to each other.

Most readers ‘hear’ written words in their heads as they read,93 and the ‘sound’ of written language in the mind’s ear has a big impact on its cognitive fluency, or how easy it is to understand. That’s why reading your text out loud, or having someone read it to you, is such a great way to review it. If your writing sounds like something a human being would actually say, your reader is much more likely to enjoy the reading experience and buy into your story or message. This applies to all forms of writing, not just fictional dialogue.

What’s more, everyday speech expresses purpose – which, as we’ve seen, is the foundation of every piece of writing. While people do sometimes chat idly to each other for fun, they engage in purposeful conversation when they want to get something done. That sense of drive and motivation is the energy that drives many types of writing, from dramatic dialogue to marketing copy.

You can get close to real-world speech patterns just by listening to what people say. Tune in to conversations at work, on the bus, in the bar or anywhere else people are talking and pick up phrases you can bring into your writing.

This obviously works well for fiction, but it has commercial applications too. For example, businesspeople describing their product or service in conversation will often use casual but colourful phrases that they would never write down. But copywriters can pick up these phrases and use them, knowing that they will resonate more with readers than business-speak.

Get poetic

ChatGPT works by calculating the ‘average’ descriptions of things across multiple pieces of existing writing. So, to stand out, see if you can find a more unusual and poetic way to describe something. It doesn’t have to be pretentious, ostentatious or wilfully weird – just a less common form of words that expresses some important truth.

One excellent way to bring a touch of colour into your writing is with similes and metaphors. These are ways to understand one thing in terms of another, using a shared characteristic as a bridge from the reader’s existing knowledge to a new idea that you want them to understand.

A simile is when you make a ‘like’ comparison between the two things. For example:

Four-colour printing is like painting with just four colours: cyan, magenta, yellow and black.

A metaphor is when you make a comparison by saying that the first thing simply is the second thing. For example:

Boredom is the deserted station of the mind: an empty platform at which a new idea can arrive at any time.

ChatGPT only knows about metaphors and similes that have already been written, and while it can see the words that have been used (known as the vehicle of a metaphor), it doesn’t understand the underlying reality (known as the tenor). So you can make your writing stand out by finding a new metaphor that will make reality come alive for your reader.

Look beyond lists

When asked to research or summarise a topic, ChatGPT naturally devolves to writing a bullet-point list, or ‘listicle’, comprising several items of equal length. This shows that it doesn’t really understand why one aspect might be more compelling or intriguing than another. Break out of this rigid structure by spending more time on the points that most interest you, or that you feel will most interest your reader. If an interesting tangent occurs to you, go with it for a while and see where it leads. If there’s a way to link things together in a timeline or story, explore that as an option too.

Say something new

ChatGPT is trained on existing text, so it can’t come up with anything new. You can stand out by offering something original in your writing. It could be first-hand research, interview quotes or just your own observations. If you conduct your own research, share your theories, methods and findings – including both what you proved and what you disproved.

Use your imagination

Everyone wants to know what will happen next. But because ChatGPT is intrinsically backward-looking, it can’t imagine the future. If you ask it to, you’ll simply get what other people have previously imagined. So extrapolating trends or speculating on future events is another way to give your text a human flavour. For example:

It seems likely that we’ll have to get used to gardening with much less water in the future. But I don’t believe people will ever want to give up the classic design of the English garden. Instead, we’ll switch out our moisture-loving perennials for more drought-tolerant equivalents, use trees to cast strategic shade and consider alternatives to lawn grass such as thyme and sedum. While they may take some getting used to, it has to be better than looking at a parched garden throughout the summer months.

Lean into your expertise

ChatGPT is, by definition, a generalist – it promises to write anything about anything. So if you’re an expert on your subject, show it in your writing. Go beyond a superficial overview and get deep into the fine details of your expertise. Work through examples or case studies in detail, giving your commentary on every step or element.

Cite them right

As we’ve seen, ChatGPT is a ‘black box’ that doesn’t use verbatim quotes or reveal any of the sources it uses. That gives you a means of differentiation right there. However, you can also go further by weaving the quotes and ideas from your sources into your own narrative, as if you were having a respectful ‘conversation’ with the authors you cite. That situates your writing as part of a wider discussion and avoids the curious detachment that characterises a lot of AI writing.

Speak to the group

If you’re writing for a particular audience, see if you can refer to shared experiences or specialist language that only members of that group will know. Referring to things that readers recognise builds trust and rapport, making them more likely to listen to what you say.

Tell a joke

While ChatGPT can produce humorous writing ‘to order’, it’s merely recycling funny stuff that humans have previously written. It doesn’t understand why things are funny, or why things are funny in certain contexts and not others. As a result, it can’t bring humour into an otherwise serious piece to dispel tension or lighten the mood.

So, without forcing a joke where it’s not appropriate, add a touch of humour to your writing to reaffirm your humanity. You can usually do this in all but the most serious subjects (for example, dealing with death or serious illness). For example:

When I started out as an editor, my boss told me that the strongest desire was not sex or greed, but the desire to change someone else’s text. He was absolutely right – but he also taught me that the best editors know when to wade in and when to sit tight. A light touch goes a long way.

AFTERWORD

Afterword

The starting point for this book was my angry rants in the pub about the evils of ChatGPT, why it wouldn’t really help people and why they should do their own writing. After a while, it occurred to me that instead of hectoring my poor friends about it, I should write down my thoughts for those who are actually interested. And you’ve just finished reading the result.

I guess this book comes across as angry and self-righteous in places. But the truth is that I don’t really mind if you want to use ChatGPT. It’s fun to play around with and it can sometimes spark ideas, even if the origin of those ideas is someone else’s writing. I just want people to appreciate the craft of writing and understand why it’s part of being human. If I’ve achieved that with this book, I’m more than satisfied.

Many thanks for reading, and good luck with whatever you choose to write.
